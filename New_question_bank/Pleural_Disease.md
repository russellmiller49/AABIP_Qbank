Pleural Disease
### Question  ###
A 62 year old man with type 2 diabetes, poor dentition, and alcohol use presents with 3 days of fever and pleuritic chest pain. He is tachycardic (HR 112), febrile (38.7 °C), and mildly hypoxemic on 2 L/min nasal cannula (SpO₂ 93%). Thoracic ultrasound shows a right, complex, septated pleural effusion. Diagnostic thoracentesis returns cloudy fluid with pH 7.16, glucose 58 mg/dL, and LDH 1,200 IU/L. Platelets are 220 × 10⁹/L and INR 1.0. What is the most appropriate next step?
 ### Options### 
(a) Begin broad spectrum IV antibiotics alone and repeat thoracentesis tomorrow
(b) Obtain contrast enhanced “pleural phase” CT before intervening
(c) Insert an ultrasound guided ≤14F chest tube for drainage now
(d) Instill intrapleural tPA/DNase immediately before tube placement
(e) Schedule next day elective VATS decortication without tube drainage
###Discussion###
Correct answer: c

With pleural fluid pH ≤7.20 and a complex effusion, early tube drainage is indicated—making choice (c) correct. The 2023 BTS guideline stratifies pleural infection/complicated parapneumonic effusion (CPPE) by pleural pH; pH ≤7.20 confers high risk and warrants prompt intercostal chest drain if sonographically safe (often with a small bore tube), alongside antibiotics [BTS 2023]. This pH based approach is supported by prior work on factors that influence measurement and interpretation of pleural pH/glucose [Rahman 2008]. Regarding tube size, analysis from large pleural infection trials found no outcome advantage to larger drains, while ≤14F tubes are associated with less pain; regular saline flushes help maintain patency [BTS 2023; MIST 1/2 era data].
Why not the others? (a) Antibiotics alone are inadequate with pH ≤7.20 and a complex effusion. (b) Pleural phase CT helps define anatomy but should not delay indicated drainage; ultrasound already shows a drainable collection. (d) tPA/DNase is considered after initial tube drainage if medical treatment fails or is insufficient; it’s not given prior to establishing drainage. (e) Immediate VATS without an initial drainage attempt is not first line in a stable patient, and many improve with tube ± IET; randomized data are ongoing regarding early VATS vs IET.
Pearl: Pleural fluid pH ≤7.20 is a decision threshold for immediate drainage when safe access exists.

### Question  ###
A 58 year old woman with community acquired pneumonia develops a moderate left pleural effusion. Thoracic ultrasound shows loculations. At bedside thoracentesis, pleural fluid is turbid. You plan microbiologic testing. Which step best increases organism yield without adding risk?
 ### Options### 
(a) Inoculate pleural fluid directly into aerobic and anaerobic blood culture bottles at the bedside
(b) Send only standard culture bottles; blood culture bottles add little value
(c) Delay cultures until intrapleural tPA/DNase has been given
(d) Perform CT guided pleural biopsy before placing any drain
(e) Rely on serum procalcitonin to infer causative organisms
###Discussion###
Correct answer: a

Choice (a) is correct—bedside inoculation into blood culture bottles increases diagnostic yield compared with standard culture alone.
A Thorax study (Menzies et al.) demonstrated that placing pleural fluid into blood culture bottles increased culture positivity to ~60%, compared with ~30–40% using standard microbiology alone [Menzies 2011]. Additionally, in MIST 1, peripheral blood cultures were the only positive source in ~12% of patients, further underscoring the value of comprehensive microbiologic sampling early in care [Maskell et al., 2005]. Broad contemporary data emphasize pleural infection’s often polymicrobial nature, supporting robust sampling (and empiric anaerobic coverage) [Kanellakis et al., 2022].
Why not the others? (b) Incorrect; blood culture bottles do add yield. (c) Delaying cultures risks sterilizing the space and reducing yield; sampling should occur before IET when feasible. (d) Ultrasound guided pleural biopsy can increase yield in select settings, but it is not required before drain placement and adds procedural steps; evidence is preliminary (AUDIO study). (e) Serum biomarkers cannot substitute for microbiologic sampling in empyema.
Pearl: Inoculate pleural fluid into blood culture bottles at the bedside—it’s a simple, high value step to improve organism recovery.

### Question  ###
A 71 year old man on 2 L/min O₂ (SpO₂ 92%) is on day 2 of antibiotics and a 12F ultrasound guided chest tube for a large, septated right empyema. He remains febrile with tachycardia; CRP has fallen only 10% from peak. Chest tube output is minimal. What is the best next step?
 ### Options### 
(a) Continue current care and re assess in 72 hours
(b) Instill tPA/DNase without further imaging
(c) Replace the tube with a 28F drain for better flow
(d) Order a noncontrast CT immediately
(e) Obtain contrast pleural phase CT now and urgently involve thoracic surgery
###Discussion###
Correct answer: e

Persistent SIRS with residual collection by day 2 meets medical treatment failure criteria; choice (e)—contrast pleural phase CT plus early surgical input—is appropriate.
Delays in optimal drainage beyond ~2 days are associated with worse outcomes, supporting the concept of early escalation when there is systemic inflammation and persistent collection despite antibiotics and a drain [Meyer 2018]. Pleural phase contrast CT clarifies residual locules, drain position, and adjacent pathology, informing either additional catheter placement, IET, or early operative management [Heffner 2010; BTS 2023]. RCT feasibility work (MIST 3) shows teams can randomize to early VATS or IET; while definitive superiority data are pending, timely escalation rather than “watchful waiting” is emphasized [MIST 3, 2023].
Why not the others? (a) Waiting risks harm. (b) IET is reasonable after imaging confirms a drainable target/loculation pattern; imaging first refines strategy. (c) Upsizing alone doesn’t address malpositioned tubes or multiple locules; larger tubes don’t improve outcomes and cause more pain. (d) Noncontrast imaging is suboptimal; venous “pleural phase” contrast is preferred for pleural enhancement and loculation delineation.
Pitfall: Continuing the same care beyond 48 h despite persistent SIRS and residual collection risks avoidable morbidity.

### Question  ###
A 55 year old woman with a loculated empyema has persistent fevers despite antibiotics and a well positioned 12F drain. You plan intrapleural fibrinolytic/enzyme therapy. Which regimen reflects the studied protocol from the pivotal RCT?
 ### Options### 
(a) Urokinase 100,000 IU once daily for 3 days without DNase
(b) tPA 10 mg then DNase 5 mg, each followed by a 10 mL saline flush, twice daily for up to 6 doses with 1 hour clamp
(c) Streptokinase 250,000 IU twice daily for 3 days with DNase
(d) tPA 1 mg alone every 6 hours until output clears
(e) DNase 5 mg twice daily for 3 days without tPA
###Discussion###
Correct answer: b

Choice (b) is correct—the MIST 2 regimen used tPA 10 mg plus DNase 5 mg twice daily up to six doses, improving radiographic clearance and reducing surgery and LOS compared with placebo [Rahman 2011].
Subsequent series explored dose de escalation (e.g., 2.5 mg tPA starts) or once daily/concurrent dosing, but these were non comparative and sometimes required escalation; they do not supersede the original randomized protocol [Popowicz 2017; 2022; Gupta 2017; Jiang 2020]. Modern statements from ERS/ESTS and BTS recommend combination IET (not fibrinolytic monotherapy) for appropriate non responders to tube + antibiotics, acknowledging ongoing trials against early VATS [ERS/ESTS 2023; BTS 2023].
Why others are wrong: (a) Urokinase monotherapy lacks robust RCT support and is inferior to tPA/DNase in evidence base; it may be considered where alteplase is unavailable but ideally with DNase. (c) Intrapleural streptokinase failed in MIST 1; adding DNase wasn’t studied there. (d) Very low dose tPA monotherapy lacks evidence. (e) DNase alone is inadequate; synergy with tPA is key.
Pearl: Use tPA + DNase together—synergy between fibrinolysis and DNA degradation drives efficacy.

### Question  ###
A 74 year old man with atrial fibrillation on apixaban presents with a loculated empyema. He has a 12F drain in place and ongoing sepsis. His platelets are 110 × 10⁹/L, creatinine clearance is 22 mL/min, and RAPID score is high. You consider intrapleural tPA/DNase. What is the safest evidence guided approach?
 ### Options### 
(a) Full dose tPA/DNase now; bleeding is rare and unrelated to anticoagulation
(b) Proceed with DNase alone to avoid bleeding risk
(c) Stop the drain and schedule VATS decortication in a week
(d) Temporarily hold systemic anticoagulation, consider dose reduced tPA with DNase, and involve surgery/hematology early
(e) Proceed with tPA/DNase only if platelet transfusion increases platelets above 300 × 10⁹/L
###Discussion###
Correct answer: d

Choice (d) is correct—bleeding risk with IET is higher in patients on systemic anticoagulation, with renal insufficiency, low platelets, and higher RAPID scores; mitigation includes holding anticoagulation when feasible, considering dose reduction, and team ###Discussion### [Akulian 2022].
In the largest series of IET treated patients (n≈1850), pleural bleeding occurred in ~3–5%; most were managed conservatively (transfusion), but risk was significantly associated with concomitant anticoagulation, renal failure, and platelet <100 × 10⁹/L [Akulian 2022]. BTS and ERS/ESTS statements recommend weighing bleeding risk, pausing anticoagulation when safe, and—if IET is pursued—dose reduced tPA in selected high risk cases, with early surgical input [BTS 2023; ERS/ESTS 2023].
Why the others? (a) Incorrect; risk is not negligible and is linked to anticoagulation. (b) DNase alone lacks supportive RCT evidence. (c) Delaying a week risks harm; if IET is unsafe or fails, early surgery is favored. (e) No evidence supports a target platelet of 300 × 10⁹/L.
Pitfall: Mistaking the IET related red tinge in drainage for hemorrhage; confirm with pleural fluid hematocrit before declaring a bleed [Akulian 2022].

### Question  ###
Your hospital has no alteplase for 48 hours. A 63 year old man with a 12F drain continues to have a large multiloculated empyema on ultrasound with persistent sepsis despite antibiotics. What is the best interim intrapleural strategy?
 ### Options### 
(a) Streptokinase monotherapy until alteplase arrives
(b) DNase alone twice daily
(c) Urokinase based fibrinolysis (preferably with DNase), recognizing the evidence is weaker than for tPA/DNase
(d) Wait for alteplase; do nothing more now
(e) Immediately proceed to thoracotomy without further drainage attempts
###Discussion###
Correct answer: c

Choice (c) is reasonable—urokinase can be used where alteplase is unavailable, ideally combined with DNase, although supportive data are weaker than for tPA/DNase and not tested head to head in RCTs [BTS 2023; ERS/ESTS 2023].
MIST 2 established tPA/DNase as the combination with randomized evidence. Subsequent observational series support reduced dose and alternate scheduling strategies for alteplase but do not change the hierarchy of evidence [Rahman 2011; Popowicz 2017, 2022]. Where alteplase access is constrained, guidelines acknowledge urokinase as a lesser alternative, often paired with DNase; streptokinase is largely abandoned after MIST 1’s negative results and immunogenicity concerns [BTS 2023]. Surgical referral should proceed in parallel if clinical deterioration continues or IET is not feasible.
Why not others? (a) Streptokinase monotherapy lacks benefit in RCTs. (b) DNase alone is inadequate. (d) Waiting risks treatment delay; escalation should not pause entirely for drug availability. (e) Immediate thoracotomy without considering other minimally invasive options may be premature in a hemodynamically stable patient.
Pearl: When tPA is unavailable or contraindicated, urokinase ± DNase is a pragmatic stopgap, but continue surgical planning.

A 67 year old woman with a loculated empyema has ongoing SIRS and a well positioned 12F drain. She has severe thrombocytopenia (platelets 38 × 10⁹/L) from chemotherapy, so IET is contraindicated. What adjunct can facilitate clearance?
 ### Options### 
(a) Exchange to a 32F chest tube
(b) Instill DNase alone
(c) Add systemic aminoglycoside for better pleural penetration
(d) Daily therapeutic thoracenteses instead of the drain
(e) Large volume intrapleural normal saline irrigation via the existing drain (e.g., 250 mL, TID for 3 days)
###Discussion###
Correct answer: e

Choice (e)—pleural saline irrigation—improves radiographic clearance and reduces need for surgery versus standard care in a pilot RCT, and is a reasonable option when IET or surgery are unsuitable [Hooper 2015].
In the Pleural Irrigation Trial, adding 250 mL saline by gravity three times daily for 3 days led to greater reduction in effusion volume and fewer surgical referrals compared with standard drain care; while small, it supports irrigation as a rescue strategy [Hooper 2015]. Case series echo potential benefit, including combinations with urokinase where appropriate [Porcel 2017; Guinde 2021].
Why not others? (a) Larger tubes don’t improve core outcomes and increase pain. (b) DNase alone lacks evidence. (c) Aminoglycosides have poor pleural penetration and aren’t recommended for this purpose [BTS 2023]. (d) Iterative thoracentesis is feasible in selected low risk cases but not ideal here given SIRS and thrombocytopenia.
Pearl: Normal saline irrigation is a low tech, evidence supported adjunct when fibrinolytics are contraindicated.

### Question  ###
A 45 year old man with a loculated right empyema needs a chest tube. He is in severe pain from a prior large bore tube on the left after trauma years ago. Coagulation is normal. What is the preferred drain strategy today?
 ### Options### 
(a) 28–32F chest tube to maximize drainage and speed recovery
(b) ≤14F ultrasound guided catheter with routine saline flushes to maintain patency
(c) 20F tube placed blindly at the safe triangle
(d) Pigtail catheter without imaging to avoid delays
(e) Insert an IPC (indwelling pleural catheter) for temporary drainage
###Discussion###
Correct answer: b

Choice (b) is correct—small bore (≤14F) drains are associated with less pain and similar outcomes to larger tubes in pleural infection trials; ultrasound guidance and regular saline flushes mitigate occlusion risk [BTS 2023; MIST 1 analysis].
Prospective trial data found no differences in mortality, need for surgery, or length of stay with larger tubes, while small bore drains cause less insertion and in situ pain [BTS 2023]. Ultrasound guidance reduces complications and ensures the largest pocket is targeted [BTS 2023].
Why not others? (a) Larger tubes increase pain without improving key outcomes. (c) Blind placement is avoidable; image guidance is standard. (d) Even pigtails should be ultrasound guided. (e) IPCs are not first line for acute empyema; they may be considered later for chronic infected spaces when surgery/IET are unsuitable.
Pearl: Pain is a meaningful outcome—small bore, ultrasound guided drains improve tolerance without compromising efficacy.

A 52 year old woman with a community acquired, culture pending empyema (RAPID moderate risk) has poor dentition and aspiration risk. What initial empiric antibiotic approach is most appropriate?
 ### Options### 
(a) β lactam/β lactamase inhibitor with anaerobic coverage (e.g., ampicillin sulbactam ± metronidazole), then tailor to cultures
(b) Ciprofloxacin monotherapy
(c) Aminoglycoside monotherapy
(d) Narrow spectrum penicillin alone
(e) Linezolid alone
Correct answer: a

###Discussion###
Choice (a)—for community acquired pleural infection, guidelines recommend empiric coverage for Gram positive aerobes and anaerobes, typically with a β lactam/β lactamase inhibitor and anaerobic coverage, then narrow when culture data allow [BTS 2023; ERS/ESTS 2023].
Polymicrobial disease and anaerobes are common yet under recovered on routine cultures; maintaining anaerobic cover initially is advised [Hassan 2019; TORPIDS 2022]. Pleural penetration of commonly used β lactams and clindamycin is acceptable based on pharmacokinetic data, whereas aminoglycosides penetrate poorly [BTS 2023].

(d) Narrow penicillins miss β lactamase producers and anaerobes. (e) Linezolid targets MRSA/Gram positives only and lacks anaerobic/Gram negative coverage.
Pearl: Continue anaerobic coverage initially even if cultures don’t isolate anaerobes—under detection is common.

 ### Options### 
(a) Amoxicillin clavulanate alone
(b) Ceftriaxone monotherapy
(c) Doxycycline plus metronidazole
(d) Piperacillin–tazobactam plus vancomycin (pseudomonal, anaerobic, and MRSA coverage)

###Discussion###
Correct answer: d

Choice (d) is correct—hospital acquired empyema warrants prompt, broad coverage, including Gram negative (often pseudomonal), anaerobic, and MRSA risk coverage, based on local ecology and prior exposures [BTS 2023; Hassan 2019; Kollef 2005].
Nosocomial infections carry worse outcomes, drug resistance, and different microbiology than community acquired cases [BTS 2023; Hassan 2019]. Empiric regimens commonly combine an antipseudomonal β lactam (e.g., piperacillin–tazobactam or cefepime) plus MRSA coverage (vancomycin/linezolid), with anaerobic coverage included or added as needed [BTS 2023].
Why not others? (a) Insufficient Gram negative/MRSA coverage. (b) Ceftriaxone lacks antipseudomonal activity. (c) Doxycycline lacks necessary Gram negative potency. (e) Ertapenem lacks antipseudomonal activity.
Pearl: In hospital acquired empyema, cover Pseudomonas and MRSA up front, then de escalate when susceptibilities return.

Choice (c)—step down to oral after ~5–7 days IV when clinically improved with >50% CRP fall and source control, aiming for a total of ~2–3 weeks, tailored to the organism and response [BTS 2023].

A 63 year old man with dyspnea and fever has a moderate right effusion. Thoracic ultrasound shows multiple thin septations and pleural thickening; bedside pH testing is pending. What single imaging related step best guides immediate management?
 ### Options### 
(a) Use ultrasound to guide placement of a small bore drain into the largest locule
(b) Obtain noncontrast CT first to confirm loculations
(c) PET CT to differentiate malignancy from infection
(d) Contrast CT with 0.5 mm slices to assess the bronchus sign

Correct answer: a

Choice (a)—Thoracic ultrasound is the gold standard point of care tool in pleural disease to identify septations/locules and guide safe, targeted drain placement, and is superior to CT for detecting septations [Svigals 2017; BTS 2023].

Pearl: When ultrasound shows a drainable, complex collection, proceed to image guided drainage rather than waiting for CT.

### Question  ###
A 68 year old man with a 3 week history of pneumonia presents with empyema; pleural culture shows a Gram negative rod. He is scheduled for VATS decortication. Which preoperative factor is most strongly associated with conversion to thoracotomy?
 ### Options### 
(a) Stage II empyema
(b) Gram negative organism isolated from pleural infection
(c) Onset of symptoms <7 days
(d) Younger age (<50 years)
(e) Left sided effusion
###Discussion###
Correct answer: b

Choice (b)—Gram negative infection is a predictor of conversion from VATS to thoracotomy; delayed referral/longer symptom duration also increases conversion risk [Lardinois 2005].

A 66 year old man with chronic kidney disease and alcoholic cardiomyopathy is admitted for volume overload and receives aggressive IV diuresis. He has a moderate right pleural effusion; bedside ultrasound shows anechoic, free flowing fluid. Initial pleural studies (obtained during diuresis) meet Light’s criteria for an exudate, but he has no fever, pleuritic pain, or leukocytosis. You are asked to interpret Light’s criteria for the team and advise whether further invasive work up is indicated. Which statement best characterizes the diagnostic performance of Light’s criteria and informs next steps?
 ### Options### 
(a) Specificity exceeds 95%, so exudate calls are almost never wrong
(b) The negative likelihood ratio is high, so a negative result is uninformative
(c) Light’s criteria are less sensitive than the albumin gradient
(d) Light’s criteria are highly sensitive with a low negative likelihood ratio, but only moderate specificity—so diuretic treated heart failure effusions are often misclassified as exudates
(e) Light’s criteria have been superseded by ultrasound scores and are no longer recommended
###Discussion###
Correct answer: d

Choice D correct. Light’s criteria remain the most sensitive biochemical method to detect exudates and have a low negative likelihood ratio, so failure to meet the criteria argues strongly against an exudate. However, specificity is only moderate; diuretic treated heart failure effusions are frequently misclassified as exudates (“pseudoexudates”). In that scenario, albumin (or protein) gradients help reclassify and align management with the clinical picture. Pitfall: Calling an exudate purely on Light’s criteria in a recently diuresed patient can lead to unnecessary invasive testing.
Why others are wrong: (a) Specificity is not >95%; transudates are misclassified at meaningful rates. (b) The negative LR is low, so a negative result is informative. (c) Light’s criteria are more sensitive than albumin gradient approaches for exudates. (e) Ultrasound scores (e.g., DUETS) are promising but remain adjuncts; Light’s criteria are still the standard biochemical approach in guidelines.

### Question  ###
A 61 year old woman with an undiagnosed unilateral exudative effusion undergoes medical thoracoscopy after two nondiagnostic taps. Multiple biopsies of inflamed parietal pleura show nonspecific pleuritis (NSP) without granulomas or malignancy. Cultures are negative. She feels better after drainage; lung fully re expands; CT shows no mass or suspicious nodularity. She is active (ECOG 0–1) and prefers to avoid more invasive procedures unless necessary. What is the most appropriate management strategy now?
 ### Options### 
(a) Empiric corticosteroids for presumed inflammatory pleuritis
(b) Close clinical and radiographic follow up, with re evaluation if features evolve or the effusion recurs
(c) Immediate repeat thoracoscopy within 4 weeks
(d) Start empiric antituberculous therapy
(e) Routine talc pleurodesis to prevent recurrence
###Discussion###
Correct answer: b

Choice B correct. After thoracoscopic biopsies yielding nonspecific pleuritis, most patients follow a benign course with spontaneous resolution; a minority (≈8–11% across cohorts) later manifest malignancy—often mesothelioma—over months. Therefore, the appropriate course is structured follow up (clinical review and imaging), reserving repeat invasive evaluation for recurrence, new suspicious radiology, or symptom re emergence. This strategy avoids overtreatment while remaining vigilant for delayed diagnoses. Pearl: NSP ≠ diagnosis of exclusion forever—watchful follow up is part of the diagnostic plan.
Why others are wrong: (a) Empiric steroids lack evidence and risk masking evolving disease. (c) Routine early re thoracoscopy is unnecessary in stable patients without new red flags. (d) Empiric antituberculous therapy is not indicated without supportive clinical, biochemical, or histologic evidence. (e) Talc pleurodesis should be individualized to symptom burden and recurrence, not performed routinely when the diagnosis is uncertain and the lung currently expands.

### Question  ###
A 63 year old woman with a history of ovarian carcinoma presents with a new unilateral right pleural effusion causing dyspnea on exertion. She completed platinum based chemotherapy 18 months ago. CXR and ultrasound confirm a free flowing moderate effusion; CT shows no obvious pleural nodularity. You plan an initial diagnostic thoracentesis and want to counsel her on the likelihood that pleural fluid cytology will establish the diagnosis at the first attempt versus the need for pleural biopsy or thoracoscopy. Based on tumor type–specific data, which primary cancer is most likely to be detected on initial pleural cytology?
 ### Options### 
(a) Lung adenocarcinoma
(b) Lymphoma
(c) Ovarian carcinoma
(d) Pleural mesothelioma
(e) Small cell lung carcinoma
###Discussion###
Correct answer: c

Choice C correct. The sensitivity of initial pleural cytology varies by tumor type. Modern series show ovarian carcinoma among the highest first sample cytology yields, often exceeding that of lung adenocarcinoma, whereas mesothelioma and hematologic malignancies have much lower yields. Counseling this patient that cytology is likely to be diagnostic on the first thoracentesis helps plan a stepwise approach; when cytology is negative in low yield primaries, escalate earlier to image guided biopsy or thoracoscopy. Pearl: Think “high yield” (ovarian, many adenocarcinomas) vs “low yield” (mesothelioma, lymphoma) when setting expectations and planning next steps.
Why others are wrong: (a) Lung adenocarcinoma has a good cytology yield but generally lower than ovarian carcinoma in comparative series. (b) Lymphoma frequently requires flow cytometry and/or tissue biopsy; cytology alone is less sensitive. (d) Mesothelioma is notoriously poorly detected by cytology; tissue is usually required. (e) Small cell lung carcinoma can shed cells but is not the top yield group on initial pleural cytology in contemporary cohorts.
