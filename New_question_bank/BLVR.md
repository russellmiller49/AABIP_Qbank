Resting vitals: BP 128/76 mm Hg, HR 88/min, SpO₂ 93% on room air. Tests: FEV₁ 32% predicted, RV 178% predicted, DLCO 24% predicted. Transthoracic echo estimates RVSP ~55 mm Hg; thin-section CT (1.0 mm) shows heterogeneous upper-lobe–predominant emphysema without bronchiectasis. He is scheduled for endobronchial valve (EBV) evaluation under general anesthesia (volume-controlled ventilation, FiO₂ 0.4). What is the single best next step before proceeding?
 ### Options### 
(a) Proceed directly with EBV placement in the most destroyed lobe
(b) Obtain right-heart catheterization to confirm and stage pulmonary hypertension
(c) Select lower-lobe coil therapy because DLCO is low
(d) Defer all interventions and repeat spirometry after 6 months

###Discussion###
Correct answer: b

In candidates with suspected pulmonary hypertension on echocardiography, confirm hemodynamics before bronchoscopic lung volume reduction—making choice B correct. Randomized EBV trials demonstrating benefit (LIBERATE, TRANSFORM) excluded significant pulmonary hypertension, so confirming hemodynamics aligns the patient with trial-like selection and safety【Criner 2018; Kemp 2017】. 

Why others are incorrect: (A) Proceeding without clarifying pulmonary pressures ignores selection guidance and may increase risk. (C) Coils are not chosen primarily because of DLCO; they also carry pneumonia/pneumothorax risks and selection is not driven by low DLCO alone【Sciurba 2016; Deslée 2016】. (D) Watchful waiting offers no diagnostic clarification and delays effective therapy. (E) Thermal vapor ablation evidence applies mainly to upper-lobe heterogeneous disease but does not bypass the need to evaluate suspected pulmonary hypertension【Herth 2016】.
Pearl: Echo-suggested pulmonary hypertension should be confirmed by right-heart catheterization before BLVR consideration.

A 65 year old man with heterogeneous emphysema is referred to your center, which does not have Chartis. Thin section CT (1.25 mm) shows severe disease in the right upper lobe; quantitative analysis estimates right major fissure completeness at 88%. He wants a bronchoscopic option with the best evidence base. What is the most appropriate next step?
 ### Options### 
(a) Refer to a center for Chartis guided evaluation to determine candidacy for Zephyr EBVs
(b) Proceed with Spiration Valve System placement based on the current CT alone
(c) Defer all BLVR because Chartis is unavailable at your center
(d) Treat the right upper lobe first with coils, then consider valves if needed
(e) Offer lower lobe thermal vapor ablation based on current imaging
###Discussion###
Correct answer: a

Choice A is correct. For Zephyr EBVs, pivotal trials used Chartis to confirm absent interlobar collateral ventilation as a prerequisite for benefit; a center with Chartis can determine true candidacy despite borderline fissure completeness on CT.  In contrast, randomized trials of the Spiration Valve System (SVS) required CT fissure completeness ≳90% for selection; proceeding with SVS at 88% would not reflect its trial based selection strategy. Thus the most evidence consistent path is referral for Chartis to assess CV; if negative, EBV therapy can be considered in line with Zephyr RCTs. 
Why the others are wrong: (B) SVS trials (REACH/EMPROVE) hinged on ≥~90% fissure completeness; at 88%, candidacy is uncertain and not evidence based. (C) Lack of Chartis locally should not preclude EBV consideration—refer. (D) Coils are an option in CV positive/incomplete fissure settings, but they are not the first step when a Chartis guided EBV pathway may be suitable. (E) Thermal vapor ablation evidence is for upper lobe–predominant disease; the question asks for the best next selection/evaluation step, not to retarget a different lobe. 
Pearl: SVS relied on CT fissure thresholds, whereas Zephyr trials relied on Chartis—use the selection method that matches the device’s evidence base.

### Question  ###
A 64-year old woman with severe COPD (45 pack year former smoker) has persistent dyspnea despite triple inhaled therapy and pulmonary rehabilitation. Spirometry shows FEV₁ 27% predicted with RV 180% predicted. Thin section HRCT (1.0 mm) demonstrates heterogeneous, upper lobe–predominant emphysema with an intact right major fissure on quantitative analysis. During bronchoscopy under general anesthesia, Chartis balloon occlusion of the right upper lobe shows expiratory flow that declines to zero over time. She asks what feature best predicts whether she will achieve lobar atelectasis and meaningful clinical benefit after endobronchial valve placement. Which variable is most predictive?
 ### Options### 
(a) Absence of interlobar collateral ventilation confirmed by Chartis assessment
(b) Presence of a CT “bronchus sign” in the target lobe
(c) Peripheral blood eosinophil count ≥300/µL
(d) Resting PaO₂ ≥60 mm Hg on room air
(e) Baseline 6 minute walk distance ≥350 m
###Discussion###
Correct answer: a

Choice A is correct. Randomized trials and mechanistic studies show that absence of interlobar collateral ventilation (CV)—demonstrated by Chartis or by complete fissures—predicts lobar atelectasis and the largest gains in FEV₁, exercise capacity, and quality of life after endobronchial valve (EBV) therapy. 
Evidence from STELVIO (NEJM) demonstrated substantial benefit only when CV was absent; TRANSFORM and LIBERATE confirmed clinically meaningful improvements in CV negative targets, while the earlier VENT trial’s modest effects were explained in part by untreated CV. Chartis guided selection improves the likelihood of complete lobar occlusion and volume reduction, and combining fissure analysis with Chartis maximizes prediction of response. 
Why the others are wrong: (B) The “bronchus sign” predicts peripheral nodule sampling, not BLVR response (not a selection variable in BLVR RCTs). (C) Peripheral eosinophilia influences inhaled steroid responsiveness/asthma traits, not EBV efficacy in emphysema trials. (D) Resting PaO₂ helps risk stratify but has not been shown to determine EBV benefit. (E) Baseline 6 minute walk distance is an outcome and safety parameter, not the primary determinant of achieving lobar collapse; responders are driven by CV status. Across trials, the consistent signal is that no CV targets respond; CV positive targets do not. 
Pearl: EBV success hinges on physiology (no collateral ventilation) more than on absolute emphysema burden—confirm with Chartis and/or fissure completeness before committing to valves.

### Question  ###
A 70 year old man with heterogeneous upper lobe–predominant emphysema underwent Zephyr EBV placement to the right upper lobe yesterday. He developed an immediate pneumothorax requiring a 24 Fr chest tube to −20 cm H₂O suction. On hospital day 7, he remains hemodynamically stable but has a continuous air leak. Chest radiograph shows a small, persistent apical pneumothorax. What is the most appropriate next step?
 ### Options### 
(a) Continue the current suction regimen and reassess in 72 hours
(b) Clamp the chest tube to test for auto pleurodesis
(c) Insert an additional apical pigtail catheter
(d) Remove one endobronchial valve from the treated lobe
(e) Proceed directly to video assisted thoracoscopic bullectomy
###Discussion###
Correct answer: d

Choice D is correct. Expert consensus on post EBV pneumothorax recommends sequential removal of valves when a significant air leak persists despite chest drainage—typically beginning after a week of continuous leak—because removing a valve reduces the atelectatic shear driving the leak and facilitates closure. This algorithm arose from pooled experience across EBV trials (e.g., LIBERATE/TRANSFORM) in which pneumothorax clustered early and usually resolved with chest drainage; persistent leaks occasionally required valve removal. 
Why the others are wrong: (A) Passive observation beyond a week with an ongoing leak is unlikely to change trajectory and prolongs hospitalization. (B) Tube clamping with an active air leak risks tension physiology. (C) Adding another drain treats recurrence or malposition, not an established persistent air leak driven by lobar collapse mechanics. (E) Surgery is rarely needed when evidence based bronchoscopic steps (including staged valve removal) are followed. 
Pitfall: Delaying valve removal in a persistent post EBV air leak can unnecessarily extend chest tube days and hospital stay; follow the stepwise algorithm before surgical referral.

A 67 year old man (50 pack year ex smoker) with severe dyspnea and hyperinflation is evaluated for BLVR. HRCT shows heterogeneous emphysema in both right upper and right lower lobes; fissures are complete. Chartis shows absent CV in both lobes. Quantitative perfusion scintigraphy demonstrates relative perfusion: RUL 8%, RML 12%, RLL 35%, LUL 18%, LLL 27%. To maximize exercise improvement and minimize V/Q mismatch, which target would you choose for valve treatment?
 ### Options### 
(a) Right lower lobe
(b) Left upper lobe
(c) Left lower lobe
(d) Right middle lobe
(e) Right upper lobe
###Discussion###
Correct answer: e

Choice E is correct. When multiple EBV eligible lobes are available, targeting the lobe with the lowest baseline perfusion (here, the right upper lobe at 8%) is associated with greater gains in exercise performance and less V/Q disruption than collapsing a more highly perfused lobe. In a study correlating baseline regional perfusion with outcomes after EBV, lower target lobe perfusion predicted larger 6 minute walk improvements. EBV RCTs provide the efficacy foundation once the physiologic precondition (no CV) is met; perfusion helps choose between equally suitable lobes. 
Why the others are wrong: (A) Collapsing the RLL (35% perfusion) risks larger perfusion loss and V/Q imbalance. B–C) Left sided lobes are not the predominant disease/lowest perfusion targets here. (D) RML is not the primary disease site and has higher perfusion than RUL.
Pearl: Among EBV eligible lobes, pick the most emphysematous lobe with the least perfusion to optimize exercise outcomes.

Which intervention has randomized trial evidence for clinically meaningful improvement in this phenotype?

(c) Thermal vapor ablation of the most diseased segments

Pearl: Do not exclude EBVs solely because emphysema is homogeneous; absence of collateral ventilation is the pivotal selection criterion.

(c) Treat the right lower lobe with endobronchial valves

###Discussion###
Correct answer: c

(D) LVRS has a different risk/benefit profile and is not first line when a CV negative EBV target exists. (E) Deferring intervention overlooks an available, evidence based option with expected symptomatic gains.
Pearl: If the primary target proves CV positive, re test another lobe—treat the first CV negative, well diseased lobe rather than abandoning valves.

### Question  ###
A 65 year old man with severe emphysema has an incomplete left major fissure (≈70% on quantitative CT). He seeks a bronchoscopic option but is told he is unlikely to respond to valves because of collateral ventilation. Which bronchoscopic technique has efficacy that does not depend on absent interlobar collateral ventilation?
 ### Options### 
(a) Zephyr endobronchial valves
(b) Spiration Valve System
(c) Endobronchial coils
(d) Endobronchial valves placed in two lobes of the same lung
(e) Endobronchial valves despite Chartis confirmed collateral ventilation
###Discussion###
Correct answer: c

Choice C is correct. Endobronchial coils compress emphysematous parenchyma and improve elastic recoil; their mechanism does not require fissure integrity or absence of CV, distinguishing them from one way valves. Best practice recommendations and randomized trials (RESET, REVOLENS, RENEW) support symptomatic and physiologic benefits, though average gains are modest and pneumonia/pneumothorax risks exist. 
Why the others are wrong: A–B) Valve efficacy depends on achieving lobar isolation; incomplete fissures/CV predict non response. (D) Treating two lobes with valves does not bypass the need for lobar isolation across major fissures. (E) Proceeding with valves despite Chartis confirmed CV is associated with poor outcomes.
Pearl: Consider coils when fissures are incomplete or Chartis indicates collateral ventilation—valves work best only when lobar isolation is achievable.

### Question  ###
Two patients undergo Zephyr EBV placement. Patient A (he) develops complete left lower lobe atelectasis on imaging within 2 weeks; Patient B (she) shows no significant volume loss in the treated lobe. Over long term follow up (years), which outcome difference is most likely based on available evidence?
 ### Options### 
(a) Equivalent survival; only dyspnea differs
(b) Higher mortality in the atelectasis group due to pneumothorax risk
(c) Improved long term survival associated with development of lobar atelectasis
(d) No survival difference, but higher pneumonia risk in the atelectasis group
(e) Increased need for re intervention in the atelectasis group due to hypoxemia
###Discussion###
Correct answer: c

Choice C is correct. Observational cohorts show that radiographic lobar atelectasis after EBV—a marker of effective lobar isolation and deflation—is associated with better long term survival. Hopkinson et al. first reported a survival advantage in patients with post BLVR atelectasis; subsequent series, including a large single center analysis, found improved 5 year survival when lobar collapse occurred, without evidence that pneumothorax worsened survival. 
While randomized trials were not designed for survival endpoints, the atelectasis survival association is consistent across cohorts.
Why the others are wrong: (A) Survival differences have been observed, favoring those with atelectasis. (B) Although pneumothorax is common early, it has not been linked to worse long term survival in these cohorts. (D) No robust signal shows higher pneumonia driven mortality in the atelectasis group. (E) Effective lobar collapse typically correlates with fewer symptoms, not increased re intervention.
Pearl: Post EBV lobar atelectasis is a good sign—its presence correlates with durable physiologic benefit and improved survival in observational studies.
