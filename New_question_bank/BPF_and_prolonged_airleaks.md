### Question  ###
A 63-year-old man underwent right pneumonectomy for squamous cell carcinoma 6 days ago. He now has abrupt cough with salty-tasting sputum, fever 38.1 °C, HR 112/min, BP 118/66 mm Hg, SpO₂ 92% on 3 L nasal cannula. Chest tube shows continuous vigorous bubbling to water seal. Flexible bronchoscopy reveals a gaping defect at the right main bronchial stump with purulent effusion seen through the opening. What is the most appropriate next step?
 ### Options### 
(a) Instill fibrin glue via flexible bronchoscopy to seal the stump
(b) Urgent return to the operating room for stump repair/reinforcement with thorough pleural drainage
(c) Place endobronchial one way valves in right lobar bronchi to reduce flow
(d) Increase PEEP and inspiratory pressure to reduce air leak and improve oxygenation
(e) Perform bedside talc pleurodesis via the chest tube to close the space
###Discussion###
Correct answer: b

In an early, central postresection bronchopleural fistula (BPF) after pneumonectomy with active empyema, prompt surgical re-exploration and stump repair with pleural drainage is indicated—choice (b) is correct. Large or central postoperative BPFs carry high mortality and are not reliably cured by endoscopic sealants or valves; contemporary thoracic series emphasize early operative management when the patient can tolerate reoperation, reserving bronchoscopic measures as bridges or for nonoperative candidates. Cardillo et al. reported high success of operative repair for postresectional BPF, particularly for larger fistulas, with bronchoscopy reserved for small/viable stumps or as adjuncts if surgery fails or is unsafe. Toste and colleagues likewise stress rapid recognition and escalated definitive therapy for central BPFs after resection. Clark et al. underscore the distinct management of BPF (vs prolonged air leak), noting its association with empyema and the frequent need for operative intervention. 
Bronchopleural Fistula
(a) Bronchoscopic glues are best for small, central defects; efficacy is limited for large stump dehiscence with infection. 
(c) Valves reduce distal airflow but are not first line for central post pneumonectomy stump failure with empyema. 
(d) Higher pressures increase trans fistula flow and risk tension physiology; lung protective, not escalated, pressures are recommended. 
(e) Chemical pleurodesis requires an apposed, sterile pleural space; active infection and unexpanded space make success unlikely. 
Pearl: Early, central post pneumonectomy BPF with infection is a surgical disease; bronchoscopy is a bridge, not definitive therapy.

### Question  ###
A 68-year-old man (40 pack year smoker) is intubated, day 8 after left upper lobectomy. He has a large continuous air leak to water seal and subcutaneous emphysema. Thin section CT (1.0 mm) shows postoperative changes without a visible fistulous tract. During bedside bronchoscopy with FiO₂ 0.4, which maneuver is most likely to localize the culprit airway for targeted therapy?
 ### Options### 
(a) Ventilation scintigraphy with 133Xe tomorrow in nuclear medicine
(b) Instillation of methylene blue onto the stump and inspection of chest tube effluent
(c) CT bronchography in the radiology suite by injecting contrast through the bronchoscope
(d) Talc slurry via chest tube while observing for leak reduction
(e) Sequential balloon occlusion of lobar/segmental bronchi while observing the water seal chamber for cessation of bubbling
###Discussion###
Correct answer: e

To localize a peripheral BPF at the bedside in a ventilated patient with a chest tube, perform sequential bronchoscopic balloon occlusion with observation of the water seal chamber—choice (e). Early series describing endoscopic diagnosis of postoperative BPFs used selective bronchography and bronchoscopic maneuvers to pinpoint the leaking airway; today, sequential balloon occlusion remains a practical bedside approach to identify the segment causing the leak so that targeted endobronchial therapy can be planned. Multicenter experience with intrabronchial/endobronchial valves similarly employs balloon occlusion during bronchoscopy to map the leak before device placement. 
(a) Ventilation scintigraphy can detect BPF but is not bedside and may be inconclusive in small fistulas or in mechanically ventilated patients. 
(b) Dye testing can confirm a central stump leak but is less useful for localizing a distal segment and may complicate subsequent surgery. (Technique described historically; limited role.) 
(c) CT bronchography can localize difficult BPFs but requires coordination and imaging; not the best immediate bedside method in an intubated patient. 
(d) Talc pleurodesis is not diagnostic and is ineffective if the lung is not apposed; it also does not localize the leak. 
Pitfall: Relying on repeat standard CT when thin section CT already failed often delays care; use bronchoscopic physiology to localize the leak at the bedside.

### Question  ###
A 72-year-old woman with a prolonged air leak 9 days after right lower lobectomy remains dependent on a chest tube. She is hemodynamically stable and would prefer to avoid reoperation. You are planning bronchoscopic one way valve therapy. Which pre procedural step best predicts procedural success and should guide targeting?
 ### Options### 
(a) Apply −40 cm H₂O suction for 24 hours before bronchoscopy
(b) Instill fibrin glue at the bronchial stump during the same session
(c) Schedule VATS decortication before valve placement
(d) Assess fissure integrity/collateral ventilation with thin section CT and/or intra procedural Chartis to choose a low CV target lobe
(e) Place valves in multiple lobes empirically to ensure coverage
###Discussion###
Correct answer: d

Before intrabronchial/endobronchial valve placement for prolonged air leak, evaluate fissure integrity/collateral ventilation—patients with intact fissures/low collateral ventilation are most likely to respond to lobar occlusion (choice d). In a multicenter analysis, Majid et al. found that fissure completeness (a surrogate for absent collateral ventilation) predicted successful resolution of prolonged air leak with intrabronchial valves. An expert panel best practice statement for persistent air leak likewise recommends confirming low collateral ventilation (by CT fissure analysis and/or Chartis) and then placing valves to isolate the leaking lobe. 
(a) Deep suction does not predict valve success and can perpetuate flow through the fistula. 
(b) Stump glues do not address distal alveolar/leak physiology and are not a prerequisite for valves. 
(c) Decortication may be needed for trapped lung, but it is not a predictor of valve efficacy. 
(e) Empiric multilevel valve placement increases risk/cost; targeting should be informed by collateral ventilation assessment. 
Pearl: Incomplete fissures (robust collateral ventilation) predict valve failure; confirm fissure integrity or low CV before deploying valves.

### Question  ###
A 66-year-old man is scheduled for pneumonectomy for centrally located non–small cell lung cancer after multimodality therapy. Which factor most increases his postoperative risk of bronchopleural fistula?
 ### Options### 
(a) Neoadjuvant radiotherapy to the hilum
(b) Neoadjuvant chemotherapy alone
(c) Left-sided pneumonectomy
(d) Age >70 years with COPD but no radiotherapy
(e) Early extubation after surgery
###Discussion###
Correct answer: a

Neoadjuvant radiotherapy is a major risk factor for postresectional BPF—choice (a). A meta analysis of 14,912 patients found that preoperative radiotherapy increased BPF risk after lung cancer surgery, whereas chemotherapy alone did not show the same magnitude of risk signal. Right sided pneumonectomy (not left sided) also carries higher BPF risk, reflecting anatomic and vascular factors. 
(b) Chemotherapy alone is not as strongly associated with BPF as radiotherapy in pooled data. 
(c) Left pneumonectomy has a lower risk compared with right. 
(d) Advanced age/COPD contribute perioperative risk but are not as predictive as prior radiotherapy in multivariable analyses. 
(e) Early extubation is generally favorable; prolonged positive pressure ventilation is more problematic. 
Pearl: Right pneumonectomy and preoperative radiotherapy are classic high risk settings for BPF—anticipate and mitigate aggressively.

### Question  ###
A 58-year-old woman with emphysema is intubated for pneumonia and develops a large bronchopleural fistula with continuous bubbling to water seal. Ventilator: volume control, VT 6 mL/kg predicted body weight, PEEP 8 cm H₂O, FiO₂ 0.6, plateau 28 cm H₂O. ABG shows permissive hypercapnia but tolerable pH. Which ventilator adjustment best reduces fistula flow while maintaining safety?
 ### Options### 
(a) Increase PEEP from 8 to 14 cm H₂O to splint the airways
(b) Increase inspiratory time to 1.5 s to improve oxygenation
(c) Reduce PEEP and tidal volume to the lowest tolerable settings and accept permissive hypercapnia
(d) Switch to high mean airway pressure ventilation (e.g., HFOV)
(e) Convert to APRV with high PHigh to enhance recruitment
###Discussion###
Correct answer: c

Minimize driving pressure across the fistula—lower PEEP and VT with permissive hypercapnia is preferred (choice c). Reviews on postoperative air leaks and BPF emphasize strategies that reduce airway pressures to limit flow through the defect, using lung protective volumes and the lowest PEEP compatible with oxygenation. Surgical/critical care reviews similarly advocate pressure minimization and individualized rescue strategies (e.g., independent lung ventilation) when needed. 
(a), (d), (e) Increasing mean airway pressure typically worsens trans fistula flow and can perpetuate the leak. 
(b) Prolonging inspiratory time increases mean airway pressure, often counterproductive for a BPF. 
Pearl: For BPFs, think “pressure sparing”: the lowest practical VT and PEEP, with permissive hypercapnia, mitigate ongoing leak while definitive therapy is arranged.

A 61-year-old man with COPD has a persistent air leak 2 weeks after necrotizing pneumonia. Thin section CT (1.0 mm) shows bronchiectasis and a loculated effusion but no definite tract. He is stable and extubated. Which diagnostic study is most likely to localize a small peripheral BPF to guide therapy?
 ### Options### 
(a) Repeat standard chest CT with 5 mm slices
(b) Bronchoscopy with dye on the stump only
(c) Ventilation scintigraphy with 99mTc DTPA
(d) CT bronchography by instilling contrast through the bronchoscope
(e) Pleural manometry during thoracentesis
###Discussion###
Correct answer: d

CT bronchography can delineate small/difficult BPFs when routine CT is nondiagnostic—choice (d). Westcott and Volpe showed that even thin section CT visualized postoperative/peripheral BPFs inconsistently, particularly when the tract is tiny. CT bronchography, in which contrast is instilled via bronchoscope and imaged under CT/fluoroscopy, has been reported to localize otherwise occult BPFs and to inform management. Ventilation scintigraphy can detect BPFs but may lack precise anatomic localization and is less practical if the goal is segmental targeting. 
(a) Repeating standard CT with thicker sections reduces sensitivity. 
(b) Dye on the stump detects central leaks, not distal segmental fistulas. 
(c) Scintigraphy detects presence more than exact segment; it’s not the best for precise localization. 
(e) Pleural manometry does not localize a bronchial communication.
Pearl: When thin section CT is nondiagnostic, CT bronchography offers actionable anatomic detail for targeted bronchoscopy or surgery.

A 70-year-old man with poor performance status develops a 10 mm circular bronchial stump defect 3 weeks after right pneumonectomy. He is afebrile but malnourished and deemed prohibitive risk for thoracotomy. Pleural space is adequately drained. Which bronchoscopic strategy is most appropriate as definitive therapy?
 ### Options### 
(a) Deploy an Amplatzer type occluder device across the stump defect sized to slightly exceed the orifice
(b) Place endobronchial valves in right lobar bronchi to reduce flow
(c) Instill fibrin glue onto the defect via flexible bronchoscopy
(d) Use a Fogarty catheter and Surgicel to plug the defect temporarily
(e) Place a straight silicone stent across the carina to bridge the defect
###Discussion###
Correct answer: a

For a large, discrete central stump defect in a nonoperative candidate, bronchoscopic closure with an Amplatzer occluder has the best supportive series—choice (a). Case series demonstrate successful closure of central BPFs (often ≥8–10 mm) with septal/vascular occluders placed under bronchoscopic/fluoroscopic guidance, with durable follow up. Long term outcomes from the same group support sustained closure in most patients. Additional reports confirm feasibility for central, round defects. 
(b) Valves are suited to distal/prolonged air leaks by isolating a lobe; they do not physically close a large stump hole. 
(c) Sealants are more effective for small (e.g., <5 mm) defects; large dehiscence has poor glue durability. 
(d) Temporary tamponade is not definitive and is prone to displacement.
(e) Stents may palliate selected central fistulas, but customizing/anchoring for a solitary stump defect is challenging; migration and secretion issues limit use. 
Pearl: Amplatzer type devices are particularly suited to round, central defects that are too large for glues and not amenable to lobar isolation.

A 64-year-old man with cachexia (BMI 16) and diabetes develops post pneumonectomy empyema with a small BPF 18 days after surgery. He is afebrile but frail, with persistent purulent drainage and a large postpneumonectomy space. Which procedure best provides durable, dependent drainage while he undergoes nutritional rehabilitation before later definitive closure?
 ### Options### 
(a) VATS talc pleurodesis
(b) Repeat bronchoscopy with sealant application
(c) Percutaneous pigtail drainage alone
(d) Creation of a modified Eloesser thoracostomy (open window thoracostomy)
(e) Immediate thoracoplasty
###Discussion###
Correct answer: d

A modified Eloesser thoracostomy provides continuous dependent pleural drainage in chronically ill patients with post pneumonectomy empyema and BPF—choice (d). Large institutional series over 26 years describe the modified Eloesser flap as a safe, durable option that allows ongoing drainage and stabilization, particularly useful as part of staged management in debilitated patients. Early PPE often features polymicrobial flora and worse outcomes than delayed cases, reinforcing the need for robust drainage and control of sepsis before definitive closure. 
(a) Chemical pleurodesis is ineffective in an unapposed, infected space.
(b) Sealants do not address the infected postpneumonectomy cavity and are unlikely to succeed without source control.
(c) A single percutaneous drain is usually inadequate for a large postpneumonectomy empyema cavity.
(e) Thoracoplasty may be considered later; it is not the initial stabilization strategy in a cachectic patient.
Pearl: For PPE with BPF in frail patients, staged therapy starting with an Eloesser window can be lifesaving by achieving reliable drainage.

A 73-year-old man with COPD has a prolonged air leak after necrotizing pneumonia. Endoscopic bronchial occlusion is performed using endobronchial Watanabe spigots (EWS) in the involved bronchi. Which complication should the team most anticipate and mitigate during early follow up?
 ### Options### 
(a) Extensive granulation tissue overgrowth causing severe stenosis
(b) Spigot migration/dislodgement requiring repositioning or replacement
(c) Massive hemoptysis from bronchial arterial erosion
(d) High grade atrioventricular block during insertion
(e) Refractory hypercapnia from diffuse air trapping
###Discussion###
Correct answer: b

Dislocation/migration of silicone spigots is the most frequent adverse event after EWS placement—choice (b). A Japanese series of 21 patients reported favorable chest tube removal rates but highlighted spigot dislocation as a common issue; antitussives are often recommended post implantation. A nationwide analysis of >1,000 EWS cases confirmed substantial treatment failure with notable in hospital mortality, underscoring the need for careful selection and vigilant follow up (with migration among practical complications). 
(a) Granulation can occur but is less frequent than migration. 
(c) Major bleeding is uncommon with EWS.
(d) AV block is not a recognized EWS complication.
(e) Regional atelectasis may occur, but refractory hypercapnia is not the predominant complication profile.
Pearl: Plan for migration: educate staff, use imaging confirmation, and consider cough suppression in the early post placement period.

A 60-year-old man, 3 weeks after right middle lobectomy, has a 2 mm central bronchial stump fistula visible on bronchoscopy. He is afebrile, pleural space is drained, and he is a poor surgical candidate. Which bronchoscopic technique has supportive series data for closing small central BPFs?
 ### Options### 
(a) Deploying an Amplatzer septal occluder across the stump
(b) Endobronchial Watanabe spigot placement in segmental bronchi
(c) Submucosal injections of absolute ethanol (0.1 mL aliquots) circumferentially around the fistula
(d) Straight silicone stent across the mainstem bronchus
(e) Lobar endobronchial one way valve placement
###Discussion###
Correct answer: c

Submucosal absolute ethanol injection around small central BPFs has been reported to achieve closure in case series—choice (c). Takaoka et al. described successful closure of central, bronchoscopy visible postoperative fistulas <3 mm using 0.1 mL aliquots of absolute ethanol injected circumferentially, sometimes over multiple sessions. A contemporary review also describes ethanol as a sclerosing option for selected small, central fistulas when surgery is high risk. 
(a) Occluder devices are more appropriate for larger central defects (often ≥8–10 mm). 
(b) Spigots are designed to occlude segmental bronchi and are used more commonly for peripheral leaks or persistent pneumothorax. 
(d) Stents can palliate central fistulas but are not typically used for tiny, discrete stump defects. 
(e) Valves mitigate distal alveolar leaks; they do not directly seal a small central stump hole. 
Pearl: For tiny, central, bronchoscopy visible fistulas in high risk patients, sclerosing injections (e.g., absolute ethanol) can be effective with careful technique and patience.
