Complications in Bronchoscopy
### Question  ###
A 62 year old man with GOLD III COPD (45 pack year smoker) undergoes flexible bronchoscopy with BAL for a new infiltrate. He is breathing spontaneously on 4 L/min nasal cannula; sedation is midazolam 2 mg IV and fentanyl 50 µg IV. After the first 100 mL lavage aliquot, SpO₂ falls to 86% and remains <90% for >1 minute despite suctioning and brief pause. Heart rhythm is sinus tachycardia. Which intervention is the best next step to reduce further hypoxemia episodes during the ongoing procedure?
 ### Options### 
(a) Switch to noninvasive ventilation via oronasal mask (IPAP 12/EPAP 5 cm H₂O)
(b) Increase nasal cannula to 6 L/min and proceed without other changes
(c) Abort the bronchoscopy and give empiric antibiotics now
(d) Initiate high flow nasal cannula at 40–60 L/min and continue with close monitoring
(e) Electively intubate and complete bronchoscopy via a single lumen endotracheal tube
###Discussion###
Correct answer: d

Initiating high flow nasal cannula (HFNC) (choice d) reduces desaturation events during bronchoscopy under sedation compared with conventional oxygen devices, and meets guideline triggers to escalate oxygen when SpO₂ falls ≥4% or <90%. A meta analysis of bronchoscopy under sedation found HFNC decreased hypoxemia episodes versus other delivery systems across studies. 
HFNC preserves apneic oxygenation, provides modest PEEP, and is compatible with ongoing flexible bronchoscopy. In contrast, simple nasal cannula flow increase (choice b) often fails once shunt from BAL and airway occupation are present; guidelines recommend supplemental oxygen escalation when desaturation persists. 
Non invasive ventilation (choice a) can support gas exchange but is cumbersome during oral bronchoscopy and typically inferior to HFNC for preventing desaturation events during sedated bronchoscopy; it also impedes instrument maneuverability. 
Elective intubation (choice e) is reserved for refractory hypoxemia or anticipated airway control problems and is unnecessary here. Aborting the procedure and starting antibiotics (choice c) addresses neither the immediate hypoxemia nor an established infection—post BAL fever is often noninfectious. 
Pitfall: Assuming a small increase in low flow oxygen will reverse BAL related shunt; escalation to HFNC is often required during sedated bronchoscopy.

### Question  ###
A 72 year old man with atrial fibrillation (no mechanical valve; CHADS VASc = 3), prior drug eluting stent 4 months ago, and a 3.2 cm right hilar node is scheduled for linear EBUS TBNA. Medications: warfarin (INR 2.3), aspirin 81 mg, clopidogrel 75 mg. He is otherwise stable. What is the best periprocedural antithrombotic plan?
 ### Options### 
(a) Continue warfarin and clopidogrel without changes
(b) Stop warfarin 5 days before, no LMWH bridge, confirm day of INR < 1.5, continue aspirin and clopidogrel, and restart warfarin the evening after EBUS
(c) Stop clopidogrel 7–10 days before and bridge with LMWH; continue warfarin
(d) Stop all antithrombotics 48 hours before the procedure only
(e) Switch from warfarin to a DOAC 3 days before the case
###Discussion###
Correct answer: b

For EBUS TBNA, choice b balances bleeding and thrombosis: stop warfarin 5 days (aiming for INR < 1.5) without bridging in moderate risk AF, continue antiplatelets (including clopidogrel), and restart warfarin the evening after the procedure. EBUS TBNA has a very low bleeding risk, and observational data show no significant increase in bleeding when clopidogrel is continued. 
Guidelines and reviews recommend stopping warfarin 5 days prior (INR target <1.5) and avoiding bridging unless thrombotic risk is high (e.g., mechanical mitral valve). 
Continuing clopidogrel is reasonable for EBUS TBNA based on cohort data. 
Continuing warfarin (choice a) exposes to unnecessary anticoagulant effect; stopping clopidogrel and bridging (choice c) increases bleeding and thrombosis complexity without evidence of benefit for EBUS. Abruptly stopping all agents 48 hours pre procedure (choice d) is insufficient for warfarin clearance. Switching to a DOAC shortly before (choice e) provides no benefit and complicates timing. Pearl: EBUS TBNA can often be done safely without interrupting clopidogrel; tailor warfarin strategy to individual thrombotic risk and confirm INR on the day.

### Question  ###
A 65 year old man on rivaroxaban (held 48 h) undergoes flexible bronchoscopy with transbronchial cryobiopsy (volume controlled ventilation; FiO₂ 0.5). Brisk segmental bleeding follows a third biopsy, obscuring the view despite wedging, 4 °C saline, and aliquots of diluted epinephrine. SpO₂ is 92% on FiO₂ 0.6; hemodynamics are stable. What is the most appropriate immediate next step?
 ### Options### 
(a) Continue repeated cold saline instillation until bleeding clears
(b) Withdraw the bronchoscope to reoxygenate and reinsert
(c) Administer prophylactic antibiotics to prevent fever
(d) Proceed directly to bronchial artery embolization
(e) Deploy an endobronchial balloon/bronchial blocker to tamponade and isolate the bleeding segment
###Discussion###
Correct answer: e

Choice e—balloon tamponade/isolation of the bleeding segment—is recommended for persistent procedural bleeding after local vasoconstrictors and wedging fail. Reviews and guidelines emphasize early mechanical isolation for transbronchial bleeding, especially after cryobiopsy where severity can be higher. 
Continuing lavage alone (choice a) risks dilutional loss of visibility and ongoing shunt. Withdrawing the scope (choice b) can lose airway control and worsen contamination of non bleeding lung. 
Antibiotics (choice c) do not control acute hemorrhage and do not prevent typical post bronchoscopy fever. 
Bronchial artery embolization (choice d) is a rescue option after endoscopic measures or if bleeding recurs; it is not the first escalation when balloon occlusion is available. 
Pitfall: Do not remove the scope during active hemorrhage; maintain wedge position and proceed to isolation (balloon) and hemostasis.

### Question  ###
A 54 year old woman undergoes flexible bronchoscopy with four transbronchial biopsies of the right upper lobe (thin flexible scope; alligator forceps; no fluoroscopy). She is asymptomatic in recovery with SpO₂ 95% on room air and normal lung exam 2 hours post procedure. What is the best next step?
 ### Options### 
(a) Routine CT scan to exclude delayed pneumothorax
(b) Routine upright and expiratory chest radiographs before discharge
(c) Discharge without routine imaging, with instructions to return if chest pain or dyspnea develops
(d) Prophylactic small bore chest tube placement before discharge
(e) Observe an additional 24 hours in hospital for delayed pneumothorax
###Discussion###
Correct answer: c

In asymptomatic patients after transbronchial biopsy, choice c—no routine imaging and symptom based follow up—is supported by a prospective study of 350 cases showing that routine chest radiography did not alter management; pneumothoraces were rare and usually small in asymptomatic patients. 
Guidelines advise at least brief monitoring for symptoms rather than routine post biopsy imaging. 
Routine CT (choice a) is unnecessary radiation and cost. Routine radiographs (choice b) are not indicated without symptoms. Prophylactic tube thoracostomy (choice d) is inappropriate when exam and oximetry are normal. Extended 24 hour observation (choice e) offers no benefit in an otherwise stable patient. Pearl: After TBLB, routine chest X ray is not required in asymptomatic patients; counsel about delayed chest pain or dyspnea.

### Question  ###
A 64 year old woman develops fever to 38.3 °C about 12 hours after diagnostic bronchoscopy with BAL (sedation: midazolam 2 mg; fentanyl 25 µg). She feels well, has no cough or chest pain, and is hemodynamically stable. WBC is 8.9 K/µL; CRP is elevated; procalcitonin is 0.08 ng/mL. What is the most appropriate management now?
 ### Options### 
(a) Treat supportively with antipyretics and observe without antibiotics
(b) Start broad spectrum IV antibiotics for presumed post procedure pneumonia
(c) Obtain emergent chest CT angiography
(d) Admit for 24 hours of telemetry monitoring
(e) Repeat bronchoscopy to aspirate retained secretions
###Discussion###
Correct answer: a

Choice a is correct. Post bronchoscopy fever (especially after BAL) is common, often sterile and self limited; procalcitonin helps distinguish self limited fever from bacterial infection. A randomized trial showed prophylactic antibiotics did not reduce post bronchoscopy fever or pyrogenic cytokines, supporting observation rather than empiric antibiotics in low risk presentations. 
Procalcitonin outperforms CRP for identifying bacterial infection when fever occurs post bronchoscopy. 
Broad antibiotics (choice b) are unnecessary without clinical or laboratory evidence of infection. CT angiography (choice c) is not indicated absent cardiorespiratory symptoms. Routine admission (choice d) is unwarranted. Repeat bronchoscopy (choice e) is not needed for isolated fever. Pearl: Low procalcitonin with isolated fever after BAL supports observation without antibiotics.

### Question  ###
During outpatient flexible bronchoscopy in a 69 year old man (40 pack year smoker) performed seated with transoral insertion and no baseline oxygen, SpO₂ drifts from 95% to 88% for >1 minute while passing the cords; ECG shows frequent PVCs but stable blood pressure. What is the most appropriate immediate step to lower arrhythmia risk and improve safety?
 ### Options### 
(a) Escalate supplemental oxygen to maintain SpO₂ ≥ 90% and continue continuous ECG monitoring
(b) Administer IV amiodarone 150 mg now
(c) Give adenosine 6 mg IV for presumed SVT
(d) Abort the case and schedule under general anesthesia
(e) Administer flumazenil 0.2 mg IV immediately
###Discussion###
Correct answer: a

Choice a is correct. Arrhythmias during bronchoscopy are typically triggered or worsened by hypoxemia and airway stimulation; the first intervention is to correct desaturation (oxygen) and continue monitoring. Studies link arrhythmia frequency to periods of maximal oxygen desaturation, and guidelines recommend prompt oxygen supplementation when SpO₂ falls >4% or below 90%. 
Antiarrhythmics ( ### Options###  b and c) are unnecessary for isolated, hemodynamically stable ectopy driven by hypoxemia and may complicate the procedure. Aborting the case (choice d) is not required once oxygenation is corrected. Flumazenil (choice e) treats benzodiazepine related oversedation, not arrhythmia or hypoxemia. Pitfall: Treating bronchoscopy related ectopy with antiarrhythmics before correcting hypoxemia; prioritize oxygenation.

### Question  ###
A 45 year old man undergoing EBUS under moderate sedation receives fentanyl 150 µg IV rapidly plus midazolam 1 mg. Within 1 minute he becomes difficult to ventilate by mask despite patent airway maneuvers; chest wall is rigid to manual ventilation; SpO₂ 82%. Blood pressure is stable. Which immediate management is most appropriate?
 ### Options### 
(a) Administer rocuronium 1 mg/kg and intubate immediately
(b) Give epinephrine 10 µg IV boluses for presumed anaphylaxis
(c) Reverse benzodiazepines with flumazenil 0.5 mg IV
(d) Provide positive pressure ventilation and give naloxone 0.04 mg IV, repeating every 2–5 min until ventilation improves
(e) Stop the case and place the patient in the lateral decubitus position
###Discussion###
Correct answer: d

This presentation is classic for fentanyl induced chest wall rigidity (“wooden chest syndrome”). The best immediate step is choice d—assist ventilation and administer low dose naloxone (0.04 mg IV, repeat every 2–5 min) until rigidity resolves and ventilation improves. 
Case reports describe this complication even with non anesthetic bolus doses; rapid injection increases risk. Naloxone reverses opioid effect and resolves rigidity; temporary positive pressure ventilation is often required. 
Paralysis and intubation (choice a) is a last resort if naloxone and ventilation fail. Epinephrine (choice b) is not indicated without signs of anaphylaxis. Flumazenil (choice c) addresses benzodiazepines, not opioid induced rigidity. Positioning (choice e) does not treat the pathophysiology. Pearl: Start with small, titrated naloxone doses (0.04 mg) to reverse fentanyl induced rigidity while preserving analgesia and avoiding acute withdrawal.

### Question  ###
An 80 year old woman (BMI 32) received midazolam 4 mg IV and fentanyl 25 µg for outpatient bronchoscopy. Thirty minutes post procedure she is somnolent with shallow respirations (RR 8/min), SpO₂ 90% on 2 L/min nasal cannula, BP 128/72 mm Hg, and a patent airway. What is the most appropriate immediate management?
 ### Options### 
(a) Place on HFNC and observe for spontaneous arousal
(b) Administer flumazenil 0.2 mg IV, then 0.1 mg every 1 min (max 1 mg), and monitor closely for resedation
(c) Give naloxone 0.4 mg IV bolus
(d) Proceed to endotracheal intubation
(e) Administer reversal for both agents simultaneously (flumazenil 0.5 mg and naloxone 0.2 mg)
###Discussion###
Correct answer: b

Choice b is correct. This pattern suggests benzodiazepine related oversedation. Flumazenil reverses midazolam and improves respiratory drive; dosing is 0.2 mg initially, then 0.1 mg every minute to a maximum of 1 mg. Because flumazenil’s action may be shorter than midazolam’s, careful observation for resedation is essential. 
HFNC (choice a) does not treat drug effect. Naloxone (choice c) targets opioids and risks withdrawal without benefit if opioid effect is minimal. Intubation (choice d) is unnecessary if reversal is available and the airway is patent. Routine dual reversal (choice e) is not recommended; antagonize the suspected agent first. A randomized, placebo controlled trial supports flumazenil’s safety and efficacy in benzodiazepine facilitated bronchoscopy. 
Pitfall: Failing to monitor after flumazenil; recurrence of sedation can occur due to its shorter half life than midazolam.

### Question  ###
A 58 year old woman with suspected sarcoidosis is scheduled for transbronchial lung biopsy (thin bronchoscope; conscious sedation). She had a prior small spontaneous pneumothorax and is anxious about recurrence. Which technique choice best minimizes pneumothorax risk during TBLB?
 ### Options### 
(a) Prefer middle lobe or lingula sampling because those segments are small
(b) Perform bilateral lung biopsies at the same sitting to maximize yield
(c) Rely on fluoroscopy for all biopsies to prevent pneumothorax
(d) Use larger cup forceps to obtain deeper bites
(e) Use alligator forceps rather than cup forceps for sampling
###Discussion###
Correct answer: e

Choice e is correct. A randomized trial in sarcoidosis found cup forceps caused more pneumothoraces than alligator forceps during TBLB; selecting alligator forceps modestly reduces pneumothorax risk. 
Sampling the middle lobe or lingula (choice a) can increase risk due to proximity to fissures and should be avoided when diffuse disease allows alternative sites. 
Bilateral same session biopsies (choice b) risk bilateral pneumothoraces and are discouraged. 
Routine fluoroscopy (choice c) may improve diagnostic yield in localized lesions but does not reliably reduce pneumothorax incidence. 
Larger/deeper bites (choice d) plausibly raise risk without proven benefit for safety. Pearl: Technique tweaks—forceps choice and avoiding fissure adjacent segments—matter more for pneumothorax prevention than routine fluoroscopy.

### Question  ###
Ninety minutes after discharge from outpatient bronchoscopy with three transbronchial biopsies, a 67 year old man calls with sudden right sided pleuritic chest pain and mild dyspnea. In the ED he is stable, SpO₂ 93% on room air, with diminished right apical breath sounds. Which diagnostic test is the best initial choice to quickly detect a clinically significant pneumothorax?
 ### Options### 
(a) Upright PA/lateral chest radiographs
(b) Expiratory chest radiograph
(c) Point of care thoracic ultrasound at bedside
(d) CT chest without contrast
(e) ABG followed by observation
###Discussion###
Correct answer: c

Choice c is correct. For symptomatic patients after TBLB, point of care thoracic ultrasound is a rapid, accurate bedside test for clinically significant pneumothorax; recent data report high specificity (≈100%) and good sensitivity (~87%) for clinically meaningful pleural separation. 
While plain radiography ( ### Options###  a and b) may miss anterior/apical pneumothoraces in supine or semi upright patients, ultrasound allows immediate decision making and triage. CT (choice d) is most sensitive but is not the fastest initial study and entails radiation and transport. ABG and observation (choice e) delay diagnosis. Pearl: POCUS is the fastest accurate test for clinically significant post biopsy pneumothorax at the bedside.
