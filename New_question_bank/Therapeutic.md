Therapeutic bronchoscopy
### Question  ###
A 64 year old man with stage IIIB mediastinal large B cell lymphoma and a 45 pack year smoking history presents with progressive stridor and orthopnea. He is on apixaban for atrial fibrillation (last dose 24 hours ago). CT chest with 1 mm slices shows ~80% lower tracheal narrowing from a smooth, crescentic, purely extrinsic mass effect; there is no bronchus sign or endoluminal tissue. On flexible bronchoscopy under moderate sedation, the mucosa is intact with a long, crescentic indentation extending to the main carina; distal airways are visible but critically narrowed. His dyspnea score is 8/10; SpO₂ is 92% on 2 L/min nasal cannula. He needs urgent palliation to allow systemic therapy next week. Which is the next best step to restore airway patency today?
 ### Options### 
(a) Nd:YAG laser photoresection
(b) Argon plasma coagulation of the indentation
(c) Airway stenting across the distal trachea/carina after gentle balloon dilation
(d) Electrocautery snare resection of the indentation
(e) Same session photodynamic therapy
###Discussion###
Correct answer: c

Bottom line: Pure extrinsic compression is not relieved by ablative tools; airway stenting after careful dilation is preferred (choice C correct). Laser, electrocautery, and APC act on intraluminal tissue; without a mass to debulk, they do not restore caliber and can injure normal mucosa. Evidence syntheses and guidelines emphasize stenting—often bifurcated if the carina is involved—for extrinsic or mixed lesions, with mechanical dilation immediately beforehand as needed. Ernst et al. and ACCP guidance describe thermal tools as ineffective for purely extrinsic lesions, whereas stents rapidly palliate dyspnea and support subsequent oncologic therapy. Ost et al. show therapeutic bronchoscopy improves symptoms and quality of life when the chosen modality matches the mechanism of obstruction. 
Pearl: Match the tool to the pathology—ablation for endoluminal disease; scaffolding for extrinsic collapse.
Why others are wrong: Laser (A) and electrocautery (D) require tissue to cut/coagulate and risk perforation when fired at normal wall; APC (B) provides superficial coagulation and will not lift an externally flattened lumen; PDT (E) has a delayed effect and does not relieve extrinsic compression acutely.

A 58 year old woman with a friable, oozing tracheal squamous carcinoma is intubated with a laser safe tube. She is on volume controlled ventilation (tidal volume 6 mL/kg, PEEP 5 cm H₂O) with FiO₂ 0.70 for baseline PaO₂ 62 mm Hg. You plan short pulse Nd:YAG laser to coagulate the bleeding, then mechanical debulking. The anesthesiologist asks about ventilator adjustments during lasing. Which immediate step best reduces catastrophic complications while allowing treatment?
 ### Options### 
(a) Increase PEEP to 10 cm H₂O to prevent derecruitment during firing
(b) Switch to spontaneous ventilation to lower airway pressures
(c) Pack the trachea with dry gauze around the tumor
(d) Raise FiO₂ to 1.0 between pulses to maintain reserve
(e) Reduce and maintain FiO₂ at the lowest feasible level (ideally ≤0.40–0.50) during lasing
###Discussion###
Correct answer: e

Bottom line: The single most important step before firing is to lower and maintain FiO₂ as low as feasible—ideally ≤0.40–0.50 (choice E correct) to mitigate airway fire. Classic safety series and consensus precautions for bronchoscopic Nd:YAG emphasize oxygen limitation, short pulses, and careful beam orientation; fires and tube ignition are strongly associated with high FiO₂. Shapshay & Beamis and Dumon et al. detail these fire prevention principles; Casey highlights practical steps to avert endotracheal fires. 
Pearl: Before any thermal energy in the airway, say out loud: “What’s the FiO₂?”
Why others are wrong: Raising PEEP (A) or changing mode (B) does not address ignition risk and may destabilize gas exchange; packing with gauze (C) introduces flammable material; “topping off” with 100% oxygen between pulses (D) increases fire risk and can be forgotten when firing resumes, a known pitfall.

### Question  ###
A 71 year old man with metastatic renal cell carcinoma and recurrent hemoptysis undergoes flexible bronchoscopy. A vascular, oozing endobronchial lesion narrows the right main bronchus (60% area). He is on 3 L/min oxygen (SpO₂ 93%). You plan argon plasma coagulation (APC) for hemostasis and to prep for mechanical debulking. The APC unit has probes of 1.5 mm and 2.3 mm and flow settings from 0.3 to 1.8 L/min. Which technique most reduces the risk of life threatening gas embolism while achieving coagulation?
 ### Options### 
(a) Use high argon flow (≥2 L/min) to clear blood and improve plasma stability
(b) Keep the probe non contact (≈1–3 mm), ensure the target is the nearest tissue, use short bursts, and the lowest effective flow (≤~1 L/min)
(c) Press the probe tip against the tumor to prevent gas tracking
(d) Occlude the distal airway with the bronchoscope to trap gas and increase dwell time
(e) Insufflate CO₂ through the scope to “dilute” any argon
###Discussion###
Correct answer: b

Bottom line: Non contact technique with the lowest effective argon flow (≤~1 L/min) and short bursts (choice B correct) mitigates intravascular gas entry, the key mechanism of APC embolism. Animal and mechanistic work demonstrate that higher flow elevates intraluminal pressure and promotes gas migration into damaged vessels; keeping the lesion as the nearest surface, staying 1–3 mm off tissue, and painting in brief activations reduces risk while preserving hemostasis. Folch et al. clarified the pathophysiology; Feller Kopman et al. showed gas emboli to the heart during bronchoscopic ablation under higher flow conditions; practical technique articles echo flow minimization and non contact use. 
Pitfall: Contacting tissue with the APC probe increases local pressure and raises embolism risk.
Why others are wrong: High flow (A) worsens pressure; contact activation (C) injects gas; airway occlusion (D) raises distal pressure; adding CO₂ (E) does not address the mechanism and may worsen pressure dynamics.

### Question  ###
A 69 year old man with a 30 pack year history presents with dyspnea and oozing endobronchial tumor in the distal trachea. You are deciding between Nd:YAG and CO₂ lasers to combine with mechanical debulking. CT shows a 2 cm polypoid lesion; distal airway is visible. You want deep coagulation for hemostasis and to shrink tumor bulk before forceps extraction. Which statement best reflects tissue–laser interactions relevant to this case?
 ### Options### 
(a) CO₂ lasers penetrate deeper into tissue than Nd:YAG
(b) Argon (blue green) lasers have poor hemoglobin absorption and limited hemostasis
(c) Nd:YAG functions primarily as a surface photoablation tool with minimal scatter
(d) Nd:YAG produces volumetric heating with deeper coagulation than CO₂, which provides shallow, precise cutting
(e) All airway lasers can be delivered through flexible quartz fibers
###Discussion###
Correct answer: d

Bottom line: Nd:YAG scatters and heats tissue volumetrically, enabling deeper coagulation, whereas CO₂ is highly surface absorbed and cuts precisely with shallow penetration (choice D correct). Classic studies and reviews detail wavelength dependent absorption: CO₂ (10,600 nm) is strongly absorbed by water with ~sub mm penetration and precise cutting; Nd:YAG (1060 nm) has deeper penetration with greater scatter for coagulation/hemostasis; argon/KTP wavelengths are avidly absorbed by hemoglobin for photocoagulation. CO₂ generally cannot be transmitted through standard flexible quartz fibers, requiring an articulating arm and rigid bronchoscopy, while Nd:YAG uses flexible fibers. These properties guide tool selection when hemostasis depth matters. 
Pearl: Pick Nd:YAG when you need deep coagulation; pick CO₂ when you need shallow, precise cuts.
Why others are wrong: (A) reverses depth; (B) argon has strong Hb absorption; (C) Nd:YAG is not surface limited; (E) CO₂ is not fiber delivered in typical systems.

Their case mix is mainly polypoid intraluminal tumors; rigid bronchoscopy is available; budget is tight. They ask which investment yields effective immediate recanalization with lower acquisition and maintenance costs. What should you recommend?

CT shows tree in bud opacities in the right middle lobe; bronchoscopy reveals thick secretions within the stent. He asks about infection risk after stenting and whether he should have been on preventive antibiotics. Which statement is most accurate?
 ### Options### 
(a) Stent associated infection is rare (<1%)
(b) Fungal ball is the most common infectious presentation
(c) Routine antibiotic prophylaxis prevents most infections
(d) Any bacterial colonization mandates permanent stent removal
(e) Infections occur in a meaningful minority (~1 in 5), with pneumonia most common
###Discussion###
Correct answer: e

Bottom line: Infections after airway stenting occur in a substantial minority (~19%), and pneumonia is the most common syndrome (choice E correct). A systematic review of 23 studies (501 patients) reported infection in about one fifth of stented patients; pneumonia dominated, followed by bronchial infection and lung abscess/fungus ball. Colonization is common and does not mandate automatic stent removal; management is individualized (airway toilet, exchange if obstructed). There is no evidence that routine prophylactic antibiotics prevent these events. Noppen et al. documented bacterial colonization after stenting. 
Pearl: Expect infections after stenting—optimize secretion management and surveillance rather than routine prophylaxis.
Why others are wrong: (A) underestimates risk; (B) fungus ball is less common than pneumonia; (C) prophylaxis is unsupported; (D) colonization alone is not an indication for permanent removal.

PET CT is avid; the patient prefers to avoid TTNA if possible due to prior pneumothorax after pleural procedures. You still have time and equipment available in the same session: an ultrathin bronchoscope (UTB; 3.0 mm outer diameter, 1.7 mm channel) and rEBUS. What is the best next step to improve bronchoscopic yield today?
 ### Options### 
(a) Switch to larger forceps on the thin bronchoscope
(b) Keep the thin scope and add a guide sheath only
(c) Abort and refer for CT guided transthoracic needle biopsy
(d) Obtain PET CT before further sampling
(e) Switch to the UTB, advance more distally, and attempt rEBUS UT localization
###Discussion###
Correct answer: e

Bottom line: When thin scope rEBUS can’t enter the lesion, switching to UTB in the same session often converts “adjacent to” to “within lesion”—choice (e) correct. Prospective and retrospective series show that adding UTB on a need basis after a failed thin scope attempt allowed rEBUS to be advanced into the lesion in ~55–60% of cases and significantly increased diagnostic yield compared with staying with the thin scope. The UTB’s smaller diameter improves angulation and distal access along the bronchus sign, enabling in lesion confirmation before sampling. (a) Larger forceps don’t fix the reach problem. (b) A guide sheath without better distal access still stalls proximal to the lesion. (c) TTNA is reasonable overall but prematurely abandons a viable bronchoscopic salvage step that may avoid pneumothorax prone transthoracic sampling. (d) PET CT has already been done and does not improve intraprocedural localization. Pearl: In a single session, “thin to ultrathin” escalation is a practical rescue that materially raises the odds of diagnosis without changing modality.

Correct answer: a

Bottom line: Emphasize high technical success with meaningful—though not guaranteed—symptomatic gains and a low overall complication rate—choice (a) correct.
