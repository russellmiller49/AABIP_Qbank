### Question  ###
A 58 year old man with maxillofacial trauma after a motor vehicle collision arrives obtunded (GCS 6) and hypoxic despite bag mask ventilation. Two attempts at video laryngoscopy fail; supraglottic airway placement yields poor chest rise. Saturation is 78% on 100% FiO₂. The anterior neck is accessible and landmarks are palpable. What is the single best next step?
 ### Options### 
(a) Perform a surgical cricothyroidotomy immediately
(b) Attempt needle cricothyroidotomy with transtracheal jet ventilation
(c) Place a second supraglottic airway and retry laryngoscopy
(d) Proceed to emergent open surgical tracheostomy at the bedside
(e) Administer paralytic reversal and attempt awake fiber optic intubation
###Discussion###
Correct answer: a

When both intubation and ventilation fail (“can’t intubate/can’t ventilate”), the recommended rescue is a surgical cricothyroidotomy (choice a). The American Society of Anesthesiologists’ difficult airway guideline identifies surgical airway as the endpoint of failed noninvasive airway control in emergencies. Why (A) is correct: A surgical cricothyroidotomy rapidly establishes oxygenation and ventilation through the cricothyroid membrane, is technically straightforward with palpable landmarks, and is prioritized in guidelines once mask ventilation and intubation attempts fail. 
Why the others are incorrect: (B) Needle cricothyroidotomy can temporize oxygenation but provides inadequate CO₂ clearance in adults and is generally a bridge when surgical cricothyroidotomy is not immediately achievable. (C) Repeated failed attempts delay definitive oxygenation and risk worsening hypoxemia. (D) Emergent tracheostomy is slower and more morbid in this context than cricothyroidotomy. (E) Reversing paralysis and attempting awake fiber optic intubation in a hypoxemic, uncooperative trauma patient is unsafe and delays definitive rescue. 
Pearl: In an adult CICO scenario with palpable landmarks, a scalpel bougie (surgical) cricothyroidotomy is the fastest, most reliable path to oxygenation.

2.	Scrase I, Woollard M. Needle vs surgical cricothyroidotomy: a short cut to effective ventilation. Anaesthesia. 2006;61:962 974. https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/16978312/

### Question  ###
A 6 year old boy (20 kg) with rapidly progressive facial angioedema from a peanut exposure is apneic. Two mask ventilation attempts fail; the epiglottis cannot be visualized. His neck is short and landmarks are difficult to palpate. What is the best immediate airway strategy?
 ### Options### 
(a) Open surgical cricothyroidotomy with a 5.0 cuffed tube
(b) Needle cricothyroidotomy with transtracheal ventilation as a bridge
(c) Emergent low tracheostomy through the second tracheal ring
(d) Repeat laryngoscopy with a curved blade and external laryngeal manipulation
(e) Retrograde wire guided oral intubation via the cricothyroid membrane
###Discussion###
Correct answer: b

In children <10–12 years, the preferred emergency surgical airway is needle cricothyroidotomy with transtracheal ventilation (choice b), not open surgical cricothyroidotomy, because the pediatric cricothyroid membrane is very small and at higher risk for laryngeal injury. Why (B) is correct: Literature reviews of prehospital/ED pediatric airway rescue emphasize that percutaneous/needle cricothyroidotomy allows rapid oxygenation when surgical cricothyrotomy is technically difficult and hazardous in small airways. 
Why the others are incorrect: (A) Open surgical cricothyroidotomy in small children risks extensive laryngeal trauma and subglottic stenosis. (C) Emergent tracheostomy is slower and often not feasible as an immediate rescue. (D) Repeated laryngoscopy in a CICO scenario delays oxygenation. (E) Retrograde intubation is time consuming and technically demanding in a crashing child.
Pitfall: Attempting an adult style surgical cricothyroidotomy in a young child can catastrophically injure the larynx—use needle cricothyroidotomy as the bridge.

### Question  ###
A 64 year old woman with severe COPD has been orally intubated for 8 days for hypercapnic failure. Anticipating continued ventilator dependence, the ICU team asks whether to perform an elective cricothyroidotomy instead of tracheostomy because her thyroid isthmus is enlarged. Vitals are stable; platelets 230 × 10⁹/L, INR 1.0. What is the best approach?
 ### Options### 
(a) Elective cricothyroidotomy using a percutaneous kit
(b) Delay any surgical airway until day 14 to minimize laryngeal injury
(c) Proceed with tracheostomy rather than cricothyroidotomy
(d) Maintain endotracheal intubation and wean sedation only
(e) Perform needle cricothyroidotomy as a bridge to elective cricothyrotomy
###Discussion###
Correct answer: c

In patients with antecedent prolonged endotracheal intubation, elective cricothyroidotomy is associated with higher laryngeal complication rates; tracheostomy is preferred (choice c). 
Why (C) is correct: A classic study found a high complication rate for elective cricothyroidotomy in patients who had already been intubated, concluding antecedent intubation is a contraindication to elective cricothyroidotomy. Subsequent otolaryngology perspectives have reinforced avoiding elective cricothyroidotomy in patients with preexisting laryngeal inflammation/trauma from the ETT. 
Why the others are incorrect: (A) Percutaneous cricothyroidotomy as an elective, long term airway in an intubated patient increases risk of subglottic injury. (B) Prolonging translaryngeal intubation adds risk without changing the basic contraindication. (D) Continued intubation is not optimal given anticipated prolonged ventilation. (E) Needle cricothyroidotomy is a temporizing emergency maneuver, not an elective plan.
Pearl: Elective cricothyroidotomy is generally avoided in patients intubated for several days—choose tracheostomy for long term access.

### Question  ###
A 45 year old man underwent emergent surgical cricothyroidotomy for maxillofacial trauma 18 hours ago. He is stable on pressure support ventilation and is likely to need 24–48 more hours of airway access. ENT asks whether to convert the cricothyroidotomy to a tracheostomy now. What is the best course?
 ### Options### 
(a) Immediate conversion to tracheostomy to prevent subglottic stenosis
(b) Conversion within 24 hours to reduce pneumonia risk
(c) Removal of the cricothyroidotomy tube and return to oral intubation
(d) Continue the cricothyroidotomy and defer routine conversion
(e) Perform bronchoscopy guided exchange to a larger cricothyrotomy tube now
###Discussion###
Correct answer: d

Contemporary evidence does not support routine conversion of emergency cricothyroidotomy to tracheostomy in all patients; if short term access is anticipated, it is reasonable to continue the cricothyroidotomy (choice d). 
Why (D) is correct: A 2022 systematic review and meta analysis found no evidence that routine conversion reduces complications; subglottic stenosis after cricothyroidotomy appeared rare in modern series. 
Why the others are incorrect: (A) The historical recommendation for universal, immediate conversion was based on early century concerns that are not borne out by more recent data. (B) No high quality evidence shows earlier conversion decreases infection risk. (C) Exchanging to oral intubation risks losing the airway in a trauma patient with difficult anatomy. (E) Upsizing without indication risks additional laryngeal injury.
Pitfall: Assuming all cricothyroidotomies must be converted within 48–72 hours—modern data suggest selective, not routine, conversion.

### Question  ###
During an ED airway course, learners practice two surgical cricothyroidotomy techniques on cadavers: the standard technique (vertical skin incision, horizontal membrane incision, hook traction, dilator, then tube) and the rapid four step technique (RFST; horizontal stab through skin and membrane, then hook traction below the incision). Which statement best reflects current evidence from comparative studies?
 ### Options### 
(a) RFST provides similar success with fewer complications than standard technique
(b) RFST and standard technique have identical complication profiles in cadavers
(c) Standard technique is faster but at higher risk of cricoid fracture than RFST
(d) RFST is preferred for elective surgical airways because it uses fewer instruments
(e) Standard technique is safer, whereas RFST is faster but associated with more complications
###Discussion###
Correct answer: e

Comparative trials in cadaver models show the RFST is faster but has more complications (notably cricoid fractures) than standard technique; thus standard technique is safer (choice e). 
Why (e) is correct: Randomized crossover studies demonstrated that while RFST reduced time to airway entry, it increased structural laryngeal injuries compared with the standard approach. 
Why the others are incorrect: (A) Complication rates were higher, not lower, with RFST. (B) Profiles differed, with RFST causing more fractures. (C) The standard technique is generally slower, not faster. (D) RFST is an emergency simplification, not an elective preference.
Pearl: Speed should not trump safety—when feasible, favor the standard, stepwise surgical cricothyroidotomy technique.

### Question  ###
A 52 year old woman with morbid obesity (BMI 45 kg/m²) and neck edema from fluid resuscitation is deteriorating with stridor. Landmarks are poorly palpable. Which immediate step best increases safety before a potential emergency cricothyroidotomy?
 ### Options### 
(a) Use handheld point of care ultrasound to identify and mark the cricothyroid membrane
(b) Prep for percutaneous tracheostomy at the bedside using Seldinger technique
(c) Obtain a contrast neck CT to delineate vascular anatomy
(d) Attempt retrograde wire assisted oral intubation through the cricothyroid membrane
(e) Administer high dose steroids and defer any invasive airway plan
###Discussion###
Correct answer: a

Point of care ultrasound (POCUS) can quickly identify the cricothyroid membrane when landmarks are difficult, improving procedural accuracy and potentially reducing complications; marking the membrane with POCUS (choice a) is best. 
Why (A) is correct: A CHest review supports handheld POCUS to localize the cricothyroid membrane during emergency airway management, especially in obese or edematous necks, to expedite and guide cricothyroidotomy. 
Why the others are incorrect: (B) Percutaneous tracheostomy is not the initial rescue for evolving upper airway obstruction. (C) CT imaging delays care and may be unobtainable in instability. (D) Retrograde techniques are time consuming in a decompensating patient. (E) Steroids may help edema but do not address imminent airway failure.
Pearl: Mark the cricothyroid membrane early with POCUS in difficult neck patients; the mark can be lifesaving if a CICO scenario unfolds.

### Question  ###
A 71 year old man (BMI 29 kg/m²) with severe systolic heart failure is ventilated for cardiogenic shock. He has a drug eluting stent placed 10 days ago and remains on aspirin and clopidogrel; he also receives a low dose unfractionated heparin infusion. Platelets 165 × 10⁹/L, INR 1.1. He needs a long term airway. What is the best plan regarding percutaneous tracheostomy (PT)?
 ### Options### 
(a) Defer PT until dual antiplatelet therapy (DAPT) can be stopped for ≥5 days
(b) Proceed with bronchoscopy guided PT while continuing DAPT, holding heparin peri procedurally
(c) Convert to open surgical tracheostomy in the OR because bleeding risk is prohibitive
(d) Stop all antithrombotics for 24 hours and proceed with PT without bronchoscopy
(e) Place a surgical tracheostomy now and restart antithrombotics tomorrow
###Discussion###
Correct answer: b

Evidence suggests PT can be performed safely in patients on antiplatelet therapy, including DAPT, with careful technique and peri procedural heparin interruption; proceed with bronchoscopy guided PT while continuing DAPT and holding heparin (choice b). 
Why (B) is correct: Retrospective series and prospective cohorts show acceptable bleeding with PT on antiplatelets; a study of patients on DAPT/anticoagulation reported low major bleeding with brief heparin holds. Bleeding risk may be higher vs. no antithrombotics, but most events are minor and manageable. 
Why the others are incorrect: (A) Deferral may be unsafe given airway needs; many centers proceed under DAPT when stopping is high risk. (C) Open tracheostomy is not proven safer for bleeding in this context. (D) Abrupt cessation of all agents for 24 h is inadequate for DAPT washout and omits bronchoscopy that reduces complications. (E) Immediate surgical tracheostomy does not mitigate antiplatelet related bleeding risk and adds transport/OR risks.
Pearl: For necessary PT under DAPT, hold heparin briefly, use bronchoscopic guidance, and plan meticulous hemostasis—most bleeding is minor.

### Question  ###
A 59 year old woman with ARDS (PaO₂/FiO₂ 130) on pressure control ventilation requires prolonged mechanical ventilation. Ventilator settings: FiO₂ 0.7, PEEP 16 cm H₂O, Vt 6 mL/kg. She is hemodynamically stable. The ICU team considers bedside bronchoscopy guided PT. What is the best recommendation?
 ### Options### 
(a) Decrease PEEP to ≤8 cm H₂O before PT to avoid desaturation
(b) Avoid PT; transfer to the OR for open tracheostomy under general anesthesia
(c) Proceed with bronchoscopy guided PT at the bedside without routine PEEP reduction
(d) Delay tracheostomy until FiO₂ <0.4 and PEEP <10 cm H₂O
(e) Switch to high frequency oscillatory ventilation and perform PT in the OR
###Discussion###
Correct answer: c

High PEEP alone is not a contraindication to bedside PT; studies report stable oxygenation with bronchoscopy guided PT even at elevated PEEP. Proceed with PT without routine PEEP reduction (choice c). 
Why (C) is correct: An ICU series demonstrated that bronchoscopic, dilatational tracheostomy did not jeopardize oxygenation at 1 and 24 hours in patients ventilated with high PEEP, arguing against categorical deferral. 
Why the others are incorrect: (A) Routine PEEP reduction risks derecruitment and hypoxemia. (B) Open tracheostomy is not required solely due to high PEEP. (D) Waiting for arbitrary thresholds delays potential benefits of tracheostomy. (E) HFOV is unnecessary; PT has even been reported during HFOV when needed, but this is not the standard approach. 
Pearl: With experienced operators and bronchoscopic guidance, bedside PT can be safely performed in patients requiring substantial PEEP.

### Question  ###
A 67 year old woman with morbid obesity and a short neck requires PT for anticipated prolonged ventilation. The team debates whether any adjunct is necessary. Which adjunct most reliably reduces posterior tracheal wall trauma and false passage during PT?
 ### Options### 
(a) Fluoroscopy to confirm wire position and tube depth
(b) Capnography alone to confirm airway placement
(c) Neck ultrasound only to identify the tracheal rings
(d) Fiber optic bronchoscopy during the procedure to visualize puncture and guidewire
(e) No adjunct is necessary if landmarks are palpable and the operator is experienced
###Discussion###
Correct answer: d

Concurrent fiber optic bronchoscopy during PT increases first pass success, allows precise puncture between rings, and reduces complications (including posterior wall trauma and subcutaneous emphysema); bronchoscopy is preferred (choice d).
Why (D) is correct: An RCT demonstrated improved first attempt success and fewer complications when PT was performed with bronchoscopy compared with landmark guided PT alone. Other trials suggest ultrasound can match bronchoscopy for some outcomes, but ultrasound cannot visualize the posterior wall during dilation. 
Why the others are incorrect: (A) Fluoroscopy is impractical at bedside and does not show the posterior wall. (B) Capnography confirms intratracheal placement after the fact; it does not prevent injury. (C) Ultrasound helps mark anatomy but cannot prevent posterior wall laceration during dilation. (E) Even experienced operators benefit from real time airway visualization in difficult anatomy.
Pearl: Bronchoscopy is the only bedside adjunct that lets you see the needle, wire, and dilator relative to the posterior wall during PT.

### Question  ###
Five weeks after an uncomplicated PT placed between the 3rd and 4th rings, a 62 year old man on the ward develops brisk bleeding around the tracheostomy with pulsatile blood in the cannula after a brief “sentinel” ooze earlier in the day. BP 88/56 mm Hg, HR 120/min. What is the most effective immediate bedside maneuver while arranging definitive therapy?
 ### Options### 
(a) Remove the tracheostomy tube and compress the stoma externally
(b) Replace the tracheostomy with a smaller uncuffed tube to improve visualization
(c) Pack the stoma with gauze and raise the head of bed to 60°
(d) Deflate the cuff and place a nasopharyngeal airway to optimize oxygenation
(e) Hyperinflate the cuff and apply finger pressure through the stoma against the anterior wall while activating emergency surgery
###Discussion###
Correct answer: e

This is classic for tracheo innominate artery fistula (TIF)—a rare, often fatal late complication. Immediate cuff hyperinflation with digital compression of the innominate against the sternum through the stoma is the best temporizing maneuver (choice e) while urgent operative control is arranged. 
Why (e) is correct: TIF carries high mortality; bedside temporization includes overinflating the cuff to tamponade bleeding and inserting a finger through the stoma to compress the anterior wall/innominate artery until surgical control. 
Why the others are incorrect: (A) Removing the tube sacrifices the ability to tamponade bleeding and can worsen hemorrhage. (B) Downsizing reduces tamponade and delays control. (C) Packing alone is inadequate for arterial bleeding. (D) Deflating the cuff increases hemorrhage risk and worsens oxygenation. Tracheostomy complication series underscore hemorrhage as a leading cause of fatality after PT. 
Pearl: A “sentinel bleed” weeks after tracheostomy demands immediate cuff hyperinflation and digital compression—then straight to the OR.
