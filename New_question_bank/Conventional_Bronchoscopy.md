Conventional Bronchoscopy
### Question  ###
A 67 year old man (55 pack years, quit 5 years ago) undergoes diagnostic flexible bronchoscopy for hemoptysis and weight loss. On room air he maintains SpO₂ 96%. CT with 1 mm slices shows a 2 cm exophytic mass arising from the right mainstem bronchus without distal atelectasis. He takes no anticoagulants or antiplatelet agents; platelets are 220,000/µL and INR 1.0. During bronchoscopy you see a friable, polypoid endobronchial lesion with contact bleeding. You plan conventional forceps biopsies and want to balance diagnostic yield against bleeding risk and procedure time. What is the optimal routine number of forceps biopsies to obtain from this visible endobronchial tumor?
 ### Options### 
(a) Three to four biopsies
(b) One biopsy is sufficient
(c) Six to eight biopsies are needed
(d) Ten biopsies minimize false negatives
(e) The number of biopsies does not influence yield
###Discussion###
Correct answer: a

Diagnostic yield for visible endobronchial cancer plateaus after three to four forceps biopsies (choice A correct). Classic prospective series show marked gains from the second and third specimens, with only marginal improvement beyond the fourth; taking more increases bleeding and time without meaningful benefit. Gellert et al. and Shure & Astarita independently demonstrated that three or four samples optimize sensitivity for endobronchial malignancy, informing modern practice. Pearl: Stop after three to four good bites unless specific circumstances warrant more (e.g., necrosis precluding viable tissue). Options (c) and (d) reflect a common misconception that “more is always better”; data do not support routine ≥6 biopsies in visible lesions. Option (b) risks under sampling, especially when crush artifact or necrosis is present. Option (e) is contradicted by studies correlating yield with the number of specimens. Practical implications: prioritize quality (target viable, non necrotic tissue) over quantity; if repeated biopsies still produce necrotic or “white” tissue, consider switching tools (e.g., TBNA) rather than simply increasing the count.

### Question  ###
A 59 year old woman (30 pack years) presents with cough and recurrent right upper lobe collapse. Thin section CT (1 mm) shows an endobronchial lesion at the right upper lobe orifice. PET is pending. During bronchoscopy on FiO₂ 0.30, you see a friable, exophytic tumor with superficial necrosis. You plan to perform both bronchial brushing and forceps biopsy in the same session and wish to maximize cytologic yield while minimizing blood contamination of smears. Which sequencing strategy is best?
 ### Options### 
(a) Brushing after biopsy improves sensitivity
(b) Brushing before biopsy improves sensitivity
(c) Sequence does not affect sensitivity
(d) Avoid brushing if biopsy is planned
(e) Brush only after bronchial washing
###Discussion###
Correct answer: b

Perform brushing before forceps biopsy to maximize cytologic sensitivity (choice B correct). In a randomized study of visible exophytic tumors, pre biopsy brushing produced higher diagnostic yield than post biopsy brushing—likely because biopsy introduces blood and crush artifact that degrade cytology. Adding brushing to biopsy modestly increases overall diagnostic yield in central tumors; the incremental value is small but real. Pearl: Collect brushings early—before bleeding obscures the field. Option (a) is inferior based on the trial results. Option (c) is incorrect; sequence matters when bleeding is expected. Option (d) discards a complementary modality; while histology often suffices, cytology can be diagnostic in a measurable minority. Option (e) is unnecessary; washing can be done opportunistically, but its standalone sensitivity is lower and timing relative to biopsy or brushing does not consistently change yield. In practice, pre biopsy brushing → forceps biopsy is a sensible order when dealing with friable lesions that bleed.

### Question  ###
A 70 year old man with COPD (FEV₁ 58% predicted) and 40 pack years undergoes bronchoscopy for a central lesion seen on CT. The tumor elevates the mucosa of the bronchus intermedius; the surface appears firm and intact with minimal irregularity. CT suggests a thick submucosal component with suspected central necrosis. He is on low dose aspirin alone; platelets 190,000/µL. You want to maximize diagnostic material while minimizing bleeding, given his marginal pulmonary reserve and the lesion’s appearance. Which single conventional sampling method is most likely to be diagnostic in this scenario?
 ### Options### 
(a) Endobronchial forceps biopsy
(b) Bronchial brushing
(c) Transbronchial needle aspiration (TBNA)
(d) Bronchial washing
(e) Curette sampling
###Discussion###
Correct answer: c

Use TBNA to sample suspected submucosal or peribronchial disease through intact mucosa (choice C correct). TBNA penetrates beyond the mucosal surface, bypassing superficial necrosis to retrieve deeper viable cells with less bleeding than forceps. Shure & Fedullo’s series highlighted TBNA’s value in submucosal/peribronchial bronchogenic carcinoma, where forceps, brushing, or washing—each limited to superficial layers—often miss tumor. Pearl: A firm, intact mucosal dome over a CT bulky wall lesion is a TBNA cue. Option (a) risks nondiagnostic superficial bites and more bleeding in hypervascular tissue. Option (b) and (d) collect only exfoliated surface cells and perform poorly when the surface is uninvolved. Option (e) remains a surface tool and is less commonly used; evidence of superiority over TBNA is lacking.

### Question  ###
During forceps biopsy of a right main bronchus tumor in a 63 year old man (CAD on no antithrombotics), brisk bleeding obscures the field. He is breathing spontaneously (FiO₂ 0.40 via nasal cannula integrated with bronchoscopy) and becomes acutely hypoxemic to SpO₂ 82%. Blood is pooling and threatens the left lung. What is the first maneuver that most effectively limits contralateral flooding and buys time for definitive hemostasis (e.g., topical vasoconstrictors, balloon tamponade, thermal tools at safe FiO₂) during flexible bronchoscopy?
 ### Options### 
(a) Instill large aliquots of ice cold saline
(b) Instill epinephrine 1:10,000 in small aliquots
(c) Inflate a Fogarty balloon at the segmental orifice
(d) Turn the patient into lateral decubitus with the bleeding lung dependent
(e) Proceed immediately to rigid bronchoscopy under general anesthesia
###Discussion###
Correct answer: d

Immediately place the patient bleeding side down in lateral decubitus to protect the contralateral lung (choice D correct). Large series and expert reviews emphasize dependent positioning as the first, rapid maneuver during major endobronchial hemorrhage; it limits spillage to the non bleeding lung and allows time for topical agents, tamponade, or escalation. Pearl: Position first, then pharmacology and tamponade—airway protection trumps everything. Options (a) and (b) are commonly used but should follow airway protection; evidence for their standalone efficacy is limited, and they do not prevent cross contamination early. Option (c) is effective once the bleeding segment is localized and a catheter is in position, but setup takes time. Option (e) may be necessary if bleeding persists or airway control fails, yet immediate conversion delays the most time critical protective step and may not be feasible. After positioning, keep the scope in the airway, suction continuously, consider cold saline/epinephrine, and advance to balloon tamponade or thermal coagulation as needed; Fig. 3 on page 4 illustrates balloon use.

### Question  ###
A 72 year old woman with a vascular appearing endobronchial renal cell carcinoma metastasis bleeds diffusely after forceps biopsy despite cold saline and three 2 mL aliquots of epinephrine (1:10,000). She remains spontaneously breathing with FiO₂ 0.30. Suction clears the airway intermittently, but oozing from a segmental bronchus persists and visualization is poor. You wish to tamponade the culprit airway quickly through the flexible bronchoscope to prevent distal flooding while planning definitive hemostasis. Which device is specifically designed for endobronchial tamponade through the working channel?
 ### Options### 
(a) Sengstaken–Blakemore tube
(b) Bougie dilator
(c) Laryngeal mask airway cuff
(d) Double lumen endotracheal tube
(e) Fogarty balloon catheter
###Discussion###
Correct answer: e

Use a Fogarty balloon catheter passed through the bronchoscope to tamponade the bleeding segment (choice E correct). The Fogarty balloon (typically 4–7 Fr) can be positioned in the targeted segmental orifice and gently inflated to compress the source, limit blood spillage, and stabilize oxygenation while additional measures are prepared. Pearl: Have a Fogarty balloon immediately available in your bronchoscopy suite. Options (a), (c), and (d) are not designed for selective segmental tamponade via a bronchoscope (GI device, supraglottic airway, and lung isolation tube, respectively). Option (b) is an airway adjunct, not a hemostatic device. Reports and reviews describe the practicality and safety of Fogarty assisted tamponade during bronchoscopy for significant bleeding, often in combination with dependent positioning and topical vasoconstrictors. This stepwise approach can avert emergent conversion to rigid bronchoscopy or surgery.

### Question  ###
A 68 year old man (40 pack years) has a new 2.8 cm, lobulated left upper lobe mass. Thin section CT shows a bronchus sign coursing into the lesion; there is no adjacent emphysema. Biplane fluoroscopy is available; EBUS miniprobe is not. You plan a conventional bronchoscopy under moderate sedation (FiO₂ 0.28) with the goal of securing malignancy diagnosis on the first pass and minimizing repeats. Which initial sampling strategy best maximizes sensitivity for malignancy in fluoroscopy visible peripheral lesions?
 ### Options### 
(a) TBNA first, then add forceps biopsy if needed
(b) Bronchoalveolar lavage alone
(c) Brushing alone
(d) Forceps biopsy alone
(e) Washing plus lavage
###Discussion###
Correct answer: a

Start with TBNA for fluoroscopy guided sampling of peripheral lesions, then add forceps if needed (choice A correct). Systematic review data show TBNA has higher sensitivity for malignancy than forceps or brushing under fluoroscopy; combining methods further improves overall yield. Needles can sample lesions not breaking the mucosal surface or located peribronchiolarly, explaining superior performance in cancer. Pearl: In peripheral lesions, needle first—biopsy/brushing can blood contaminate subsequent aspirations. Options (b), (e) have poor standalone sensitivity for focal nodules/masses and add little when used alone. Option (c) underperforms TBNA and is more vulnerable to bleeding related smear degradation. Option (d) is reasonable for benign processes but often yields less malignant diagnostic material than TBNA. Practical tip: when multiple tools are used, perform TBNA before forceps/brushing to avoid blood filled aspirations; this sequencing is emphasized in conventional approaches.

### Question  ###
A 61 year old woman (never smoker) is referred for bronchoscopic sampling of a 1.6 cm left upper lobe nodule discovered on lung cancer screening. She is a potential surgical candidate (PFTs: FEV₁ 92% predicted, DLCO 85%). High resolution CT with 1 mm slices demonstrates a tubular airway leading directly into the nodule, which is part solid with a 9 mm solid core. No calcification is present; the lesion lies 4.8 cm from the hilum. PET has not yet been performed. You plan conventional bronchoscopy with fluoroscopy. Which radiographic feature most strongly predicts successful transbronchial sampling?
 ### Options### 
(a) Spiculated margins
(b) Presence of a “bronchus sign” leading into the lesion
(c) Distance >3 cm from hilum
(d) Popcorn calcification
(e) Pure ground glass attenuation
###Discussion###
Correct answer: b

The bronchus sign on thin section CT is the strongest predictor of bronchoscopic sampling success (choice B correct). When a bronchus leads into or lies within a lesion, instruments can reach the target airway more reliably, improving diagnostic yield, even for small nodules. Pearl: Always scrutinize the thin section CT for a bronchus sign before planning tools and approach. Option (a) may raise malignancy probability but does not by itself predict bronchoscopic access. Option (c) (distance from hilum) has mixed predictive value and is less important than airway lesion continuity. Option (d) suggests hamartoma but is not a predictor of sampling success. Option (e) (pure ground glass) is associated with lower bronchoscopic yields compared with solid/part solid lesions; reliance on bronchoscopy may be misplaced for pure GGNs. Thin section correlation studies (including EBUS GS contexts) consistently identify the bronchus sign as a key predictor for successful transbronchial diagnosis.

### Question  ###
A 62 year old man with a prior drug eluting coronary stent (placed 2 years ago) is scheduled for elective flexible bronchoscopy with endobronchial forceps biopsies of a right mainstem tumor seen on CT. He takes aspirin 81 mg daily for secondary prevention; no other antiplatelets or anticoagulants. Labs: platelets 175,000/µL; INR 1.0; creatinine normal. He has no bleeding history. You want to minimize bleeding risk without increasing cardiovascular risk. What is the most appropriate antiplatelet management?
 ### Options### 
(a) Transfuse platelets to >100,000/µL before biopsy
(b) Stop aspirin 7 days prior to biopsy
(c) Continue low dose aspirin without interruption
(d) Stop aspirin and substitute clopidogrel
(e) Proceed only if INR ≤ 1.0 and aspirin is withheld
###Discussion###
Correct answer: c

Continue low dose aspirin for diagnostic flexible bronchoscopy with planned forceps biopsies (choice C correct). Contemporary guidelines support proceeding without stopping aspirin when platelet count and coagulation parameters are acceptable; withholding aspirin increases cardiovascular risk without clearly reducing clinically significant bleeding. Pearl: Aspirin is generally continued; clopidogrel is not. Option (a) is unnecessary; his platelet count is adequate. Option (b) is not recommended for routine diagnostic bronchoscopy and exposes him to stent related risk. Option (d) worsens bleeding risk and provides no benefit. Option (e) conflates warfarin management (INR) with aspirin; INR is already normal and aspirin need not be withheld. In contrast, agents like clopidogrel are typically interrupted for biopsy, and anticoagulants require drug specific management. These recommendations align with BTS guidance for bronchoscopy and ACCP guidance for peri procedural antithrombotic management.

### Question  ###
A 66 year old woman with a 3.2 cm fluoroscopy visible right upper lobe mass is scheduled for peripheral TBNA and adjunctive sampling. The oncologist requests sufficient material for reflex next generation sequencing (EGFR/ALK/ROS1 and others) and PD L1 immunohistochemistry if cancer is confirmed. Rapid on site evaluation is unavailable today. To maximize downstream adequacy for comprehensive genotyping from TBNA, how should you process the first needle passes?
 ### Options### 
(a) Air dry all smears; discard residual material
(b) Rinse needle in heparinized saline; avoid formalin
(c) Prepare alcohol fixed smears only; no cell block
(d) Prepare direct smears and flush residual needle contents into 10% neutral buffered formalin to create a cell block
(e) Spray fix smears and place residual in RBC lysis buffer
###Discussion###
Correct answer: d

Combine direct smears with formalin fixed cell block processing of TBNA material to support comprehensive genotyping (choice D correct). In a contemporary series of peripheral lung cancers sampled by TBNA under fluoroscopic guidance, smear cytology plus cell block (by flushing residual material into 10% neutral buffered formalin) enabled complete molecular profiling—including PD L1—in the great majority of cases. Pearl: Always save residual TBNA material in formalin for a cell block—don’t waste it. Option (a) squanders cellular material and compromises IHC/molecular tests. Option (b) avoids formalin needed for robust IHC/PD L1 and many NGS workflows. Option (c) omits the cell block that laboratories rely on for ancillary studies. Option (e) uses a non validated buffer for comprehensive pathology; downstream performance is uncertain. While newer tools (e.g., transbronchial cryobiopsy) can yield large tissue fragments, TBNA with proper processing remains adequate for most genotyping needs when performed thoughtfully.

### Question  ###
A 58 year old man with a 4.5 cm right upper lobe primary mass has CT enlarged mediastinal nodes: subcarinal 2.2 cm (station 7), right paratracheal 1.8 cm (4R), and left paratracheal 1.0 cm (4L). PET shows uptake in the primary and both 7 and 4R. EBUS is unavailable today. Under moderate sedation (SpO₂ 95% on FiO₂ 0.28), you plan conventional TBNA (cTBNA) to stage him before definitive therapy. To maximize first pass yield and minimize contamination, which target should you sample first?
 ### Options### 
(a) 4L (1.0 cm)
(b) 4R (1.8 cm)
(c) Alternate punctures of 4L and 4R
(d) Sample the primary tumor before any nodes
(e) 7 (2.2 cm)
###Discussion###
Correct answer: e

Sample the subcarinal (station 7) 2.2 cm node first (choice E correct). cTBNA sensitivity increases with node size and is generally higher at subcarinal and right paratracheal sites; larger targets yield more diagnostic aspirates. Meta analytic and staging data report robust accuracy for cTBNA when nodes are ≥2 cm, with diminishing gains beyond ~2.5 cm. Pearl: In cTBNA, big and central first—subcarinal or 4R—before smaller or left sided nodes. Option (a) begins with the smallest, least favorable site, lowering first pass success. Option (b) is reasonable but smaller than 7; starting at 7 better maximizes yield. Option (c) adds no advantage and complicates workflow. Option (d) risks tumor cell contamination of the needle; guidance emphasizes sampling mediastinal nodes before the primary to preserve specificity. Practically, perform ≥4 passes per station as yield rises through the fourth pass; if negative at 4L and 4R after adequate sampling, proceed to alternative modalities when available.
